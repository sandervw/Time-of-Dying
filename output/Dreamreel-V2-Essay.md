# Dreamreel — Essay Blobs

## 1. ESSAY

I, who once kept a hedgerow cottage within an hour's plodding of Sulky Wend, had not crossed its sagging gatepost in some seventeen winters, and I confess the prospect now finds me less stirred than a careful man should be. In those years I went by another name; the villagers, when they spoke of me at all, called me a quiet tenant and left it at that. I rarely entered the village proper, preferring to take my bread and my candles from a roadside grocer two miles off, who never asked after my hands.

The errand that brings me back is, I suppose, a debt. So I name it, at least, for the word sits more comfortably in the mouth than its truer cognates. There are those among my old colleagues who would call this work cowardice dressed in a scholar's cloak, and to them I would offer no quarrel; howbeit, the line between cowardice and craft is, I think, finer than the moralists allow. My own reflection, when I press it on the matter, declines to elaborate.

The Hooded Pillow squats at the bend of the road like a sated tick. There, then, I begin.

## 4. ESSAY

The Hooded Pillow was, in its better century, a lodging for the under-staff of the Tower of the Attercop, those scriveners and still-room hands too junior to keep rooms within the silked walls themselves. The ledger on the bedmeister's desk is bound in attercop silk; this I noted in passing as I crossed the common room. Such a binding was a former gift from the Tower mancers, given as one might give a hound a bone for good service, and it has outlasted both giver and given.

I find I cannot help surveying the residents as material. The boy with the unbroken neck, the girl with her clean teeth and steady hand at the cup, the old woman whose eyes drift past me as if I were a draft — each is tissue, each is future stock, and each, methinks, would be appalled to know how the scholar's gaze disrobes them. The line between hospitality and harvest has ever been thinner than guests imagine. As to the funeral rite I heard murmured on the stair, I will say nothing further yet; some matters keep better unspoken.

The bedmeister behind his curtain will be a customer, not a colleague. The distinction matters.

## 6. ESSAY

When the negotiation seemed ripe, I stepped from the alcove where I had stood and presented myself, in the temperate language of the trade, as a wandering scholar with some small competence in the dream-mancies. Dream-mancy, I allowed, is a dead specialism; no licensed practitioner of it has been registered upon the Road of Graves these eighty winters. I work, I said, by barter and by oath, never by coin, for coin is a poor binder of the kind of promises my craft requires.

It is an old negotiator's trick to let the other party set the terms first, and I employed it without shame. Weirmoth, the spear-for-hire at the bar, named his price; the bedmeister, behind the curtain, named the village's pooled purse. I named, in return, only my willingness to ride at dawn for the bluff above Sulky Wend. Howbeit, I should note that Noseman's eyes flickered when he heard my voice, in the manner of a man who half-remembers a face from a fever-dream and chooses not to ask. His silence afterward was, I think, the politer half of the bargain.

## 9. ESSAY

The Tower of the Attercop stands upon its bluff like a grey nail driven into the morning, silked over and patient, and the dawn light gives the spider-skin upon it a soft, almost amniotic glow. Generations of attercops have woven and rewoven that exterior since the place was sealed, and the silk patina is now thicker, in places, than the stone beneath. A pretty image, were one not aware of what waits within.

The seal upon its door is the work of one Neinstitch, an old colleague whose specialty lay in sutured locks: doors that answer only to a whispered word, refusing brute force as a fastidious woman refuses a coarse jest. I have always held that the real strength of mancy is knowledge, not violence; the spear and the sword are vulgar instruments compared to the proper syllable, properly placed. Weirmoth glances at me sidelong, uneasy that I carry no visible weapon; I do not enlighten him.

As to the word "unsealing," it implies more violence than the morning's labor will, in fact, require. Of what waits beyond the seal I will say nothing further to the man who walks beside me. There is a kindness, sometimes, in withholding.

## 14. ESSAY

The gas, I observe, has preserved certain tissues with greater fidelity than others. Lung holds best, being airy and porous; marrow keeps its color; the eye, surprisingly, weathers the centuries with only a clouding of the cornea, so that the dead invaders below now stare upward with a mild, almost philosophical disappointment. Liver and gut, by contrast, have gone to a thin black paste between the ribs. There are reasons for this distribution, all of them written in the treatises, none of them edifying.

A lectern stands in the antechamber with three texts laid open at the same page. I find this arresting. The hand that opened them was interrupted at a point of comparison, and never returned. The preserved ledger beside the lectern lists names beside duty-hours, all of them long past their reckoning; I read the column twice, slowly, and recognize more entries than I would prefer. Past the lectern, woven across the throat of the lower passage, hangs the muscle-wall: a great pale weave of preserved tissue knitted bench to bench, rib to rib, doorway to doorway. It is, I think, both seal and tumor, the Tower's last immune response to its own contagion.

The name my old colleagues used for me is on that ledger. I will not write it here.

## 16. ESSAY

The Scriptorium opens in three floors about a central balcony, and where once a false sun globe hung from the vault to lend reading-light, now only a verdigrised husk swings on its chain, dull as a bruised plum. The shelves themselves are silked, codices moldering in their stalls, the gas having patinaed every cover to a soft greenish skin. Beautiful, in its way. I lose minutes there, more than is professional, and find that my hand has settled, of its own accord, upon a copy of Potions and Poisons of Eleven Reagents.

These texts, I think, are worth more than the village from which my fee is drawn; certainly they are worth more than the man who watches from the balcony rail, fingering his spear. I do not say so. Nor do I mention that the treatise in my hands bears, in its colophon, three names, and that one of those names was once mine. Weirmoth asks, with a roadman's bluntness, where I came by my familiarity with this room. I find a polite silence sufficient answer, and turn the page.

## 18. ESSAY

The cauldron in the lower chamber is stoppered, true, and yet it leaks. Stoppered things leak; this is a principle older than mancy. The breach-poison within has been seeping these three thousand-odd nights, slow as marrow drawn through linen, and its elements have at last reached the sleepers of Sulky Wend by the only road open to them. They call it the Dreamreel; it is, more properly, a chronic effluvium of compound sevenfold, manifesting as shared vision in habituated dreamers.

To brew the antidote — what I shall call, for brevity, the Lidd Decoction — one requires eleven reagents. Ten I can name without hesitation: Widowwine, Priest's Quicklime, Honey of the Dead Hive, Sap of the Hanging Birch, ferment of cracked beetle, salt of vigil, ash of a name spoken thrice, raw oneirocyte, Tower-vinegar, and a pinch of the patron's own marrow. The eleventh, called in the older books a Respiration from a Silver Dream, must be drawn from a memory not one's own, and to draw it requires a second potion that ushers the drinker into that memory as a witness. The drinker must be unhabituated to the Dreamreel, lest the gas claim him. That Weirmoth, by his own boast, never dreams is, I would hazard, a happy accident — though I confess the word "accident" sits uneasily on the tongue.

## 21. ESSAY

I stand over Weirmoth where he sleeps at the brewing-bench, his lips working at names which do not belong to him, and the sight returns me to a night I had thought safely interred. Lo, the rival cabal at the gates; lo, my colleagues moving through their stations with the bored composure of men untying boots at the end of a long day. Whitlow stirring the cauldron, Sourbell at the lectern with the breach-formula, Alder counting the chairs as if attendance were the night's chief concern.

It was given to me, then, to set my hand upon the lever; and given to me, also, to seal the lid; and given to me, last, to draw the door behind me and to whisper the word that closed the seal, while my colleagues continued their stations within. I have often, in the years between, called the act cowardice, and I call it so still. Crime, I think, is a graver word, and I have not earned it. There was, in the doing, no grief; only a cerebral, almost taxonomic relief, as one feels at the closing of a long account.

In parallel, my one good hand and one prosthetic carry the Lidd Decoction toward its proper steeping. The brewing wants no commentary. Howbeit, the name of the decoction — but I have said enough on that head.

## 26. ESSAY

Weirmoth, before the threshold of the Cauldron Chamber, declares himself bound by the bargain still and disinclined to interfere; I commend him, in silence, for the rare professional courtesy of a man who keeps his contract past the point of its profit. The chamber within is colder than the still-room, and the cauldron squats at its center like a great patient liver, the lid stoppered yet weeping its slow, sevenfold weep. I empty the Lidd Decoction into it. There is a hiss, a brief plume of greenish steam, and then a long sigh, as of a long-held breath let out at last.

I intone the deadspeech in the formal mode, carrying the syllables until the contents of the cauldron resolve to a thick, spent muck and at last go still. Then I speak my closing argument. The cadence is archaic, the register formal; I am not pleading, I am stating terms, as I would have stated them at any assize of my old order. I yield up the stolen reagents, jar by jar, in the order taken, and I leave the pendant against my ribs where the dead invader bequeathed it. Some debts, I think, are paid by the carrying, not by the surrender.
