# The Song You Carried In: Scene 1

## Full Scene

The slope led down at a gentle grade, and Igon followed it. Rounded sandstone walls closed in on either side, smooth as villa stucco, pale ochre where the glowbugs lit them from their lantern at his hip. The glowbugs swayed and threw amber shadows that climbed the ceiling in slow arcs. Beneath his boots the dust was thick and white. He tasted it: powdered limestone, with something sweeter underneath. Centuries of ancestral incense, calcified to mineral grit.

"Not a soul in three hundred years," said Igon to the dark. His voice rang inside the helm, too loud, and he let it settle before continuing. "Not one necromancer brave enough to crack the seal. And here walks Igon of Ald Soad, pilgrim of the wind, with nothing but good cheer and a glass sword." His fingers found the claymore's grip as he said it, and left it again.

He laughed. The tomb swallowed it.

The descent steepened. Igon braced his stride, one gauntlet trailing the wall for balance, and felt the sandstone grow colder under his fingers. The silver plume of his helm scraped the ceiling; he ducked, cursed mildly, and kept on.

The slope ended at a door.

It was crystal. Translucent, milky, veined with fractures fine as old pond ice. No handle, no hinge, no rune cut into the frame. Igon studied it, then rapped it with his knuckles: solid. He set his shoulder against it and shoved. Nothing. He drew back and considered.

"Well," he said. "You've kept them out a long while."

He pressed his ear to the crystal and listened. Silence, but not the silence of an empty room: a held silence, as though the air on the other side had drawn a breath and not released it. His jaw tightened. He waited. Nothing stirred.

Igon straightened. Disappointment sat on him briefly, and he shrugged it off as a dog shrugs rain. He hummed. A low, wandering tune, half a hymn to the wind and half something older that lived in his throat before he could name it. The sound buzzed inside his visor, warm and tuneless and his own. He leaned back to the crystal, still humming, and pressed his gauntlet flat against it.

The crystal shivered under his palm.

He sprang back. His hand found the claymore's grip. The door trembled in its frame, a sustained vibration that climbed in pitch until the fracture-veins lit from within, white and briefly blinding. Then it slid open, grinding into the wall with the sound of sand pouring through a glass.

He stared at the dark beyond the threshold, one hand still on the grip.

"Hah," he said. "So that's the trick of it."

He stepped through. The air beyond was stale, sweet, thick as wool; old incense calcified to powder that coated his tongue. Behind him the crystal door slid shut with a soft grinding sigh.

Igon turned, hummed two notes, and the door opened. He let it close again.

"Good," he said. "Very good. The wind provides."

He unsheathed the blue glass claymore. The blade caught the lantern's glow and burned cold in the dark. Igon faced the passage ahead, settled his grip, and walked forward.
