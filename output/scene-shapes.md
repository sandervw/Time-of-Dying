# Scene Shapes — Pre-1985 Weird / Fantasy / SF

A working catalog of distinct scene "shapes" recognizable across the canonical pre-1985 weird and fantastical traditions (Clark Ashton Smith, Robert E. Howard, H.P. Lovecraft, Lord Dunsany, Jack Vance, Mervyn Peake, Michael Moorcock, William Hope Hodgson, et al.). Each entry sketches the shape's structural logic, surface signature, narrative purpose, typical placement in a larger story, and a representative example or two.

The point of the list is variety. Most stories interleave these; a story built entirely on Shape 1 (Dramatic Arc) feels relentlessly schematic, while a story that varies shapes scene-to-scene breathes.

---

## 1. Dramatic Arc

**Structure.** Characters with conflicting desires collide; something gives way by the close. Setup → escalation → turn. The collision can run physical (combat, chase, escape, ascent), verbal (negotiation, interrogation, courtly fencing), or mixed.

**Surface signature.** Active protagonist, visible antagonist, a turn near the end that answers the scene's question — often ironically. In physical mode: short sentences, concrete verbs, weapons and terrain foregrounded. In verbal mode: long exchanges, mannered diction, status and information moving through dialogue. Interiority is sparse either way.

**Purpose.** Advances plot in the most legible way. Delivers concrete change — a fight resolves, a deal is struck, a secret is forced.

**Placement.** The workhorse. Climaxes typically land here.

**Examples.** Howard's Pictish raid in "Beyond the Black River" (physical); Cugel haggling with Iucounu in Vance (verbal); the tower assault in "The Tower of the Elephant" (mixed).

---

## 2. Revelation

**Structure.** Accretion. Detail layers on detail, document on document, hint on hint, until a single epiphany breaks. The "conflict" is between the protagonist and what they are about to understand; the climax is the moment of comprehension, not action.

**Surface signature.** Often heavy with documents, fragments, overheard talk, half-glimpsed objects. The final beat is a sentence of recognition.

**Purpose.** Delivers worldbuilding and horror simultaneously. Reframes everything that came before. Cracks the protagonist's worldview; the reader feels the crack.

**Placement.** Pivots and climaxes. Frequently the closing scene of a story; sometimes the inciting scene that sets the rest in motion.

**Examples.** "The Call of Cthulhu" (the assembled documents converge on the cult).

---

## 3. Reverie

**Structure.** Causality loosens; imagery and atmosphere become the content. The scene moves by sensory drift, by associative succession, or by procession of discrete tableaux — figures in a parade, rooms in a palace, dream-images down a corridor. The protagonist is observer or witness, not agent.

**Surface signature.** Time slackens. Long descriptive cadence or list-like progression. Strong visual and aural specificity per unit. Loosened logic when dream-pitched — shifting geometry, archetypal imagery. Closure is abrupt or quiet: waking, dissolution, silence.

**Purpose.** Establishes a world's emotional weather; conveys scale and strangeness in compressed form. Slows pace so subsequent dramatic scenes hit harder. Smuggles thematic or prophetic material past the reader's rational defenses.

**Placement.** Openings, transitions, hinges. Often just before a Revelation or just after a Dramatic Arc. Rarely a climax.

**Examples.** Smith's "The City of the Singing Flame" opening (drift); his "Empire of the Necromancers" (procession); Lovecraft's "The Dreams in the Witch House" (oneiric).

---

## 4. Embedded Tale

**Structure.** The scene is, or is dominated by, a story-within-the-story — a manuscript read aloud, a letter quoted in full, an oral account delivered to a listener, a recording played back. The frame characters listen; the embedded teller speaks; the embedded story has its own miniature arc.

**Surface signature.** Shift in voice and often in register. Quoted blocks, italics, archaic diction, or a frame character introducing and closing the inner tale. The outer scene barely moves; the inner one supplies the events.

**Purpose.** Imports backstory or remote events with their own atmosphere intact. Layers epistemic distance — events are real, but mediated, and the mediation is itself a clue. Often a Revelation in disguise.

**Placement.** Early, to seed mystery; or mid-story, to deepen it. Rarely a climax in itself, but frequently the load-bearing scene that *enables* a later climax.

**Examples.** The manuscripts in Hodgson's *The House on the Borderland*; the tales told around the fire in Howard's "The People of the Black Circle."
