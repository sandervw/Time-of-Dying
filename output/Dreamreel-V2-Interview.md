# Dreamreel — Interview Blobs

## 5. INTERVIEW (4) — Bedmeister Noseman, under eaves, behind the curtain

**The village.**
Aye, well. The village ails, sir, you see it on the faces. Not a cough, not a fever. It's a worry that crawls in at sleep's edge, every blessed night, and sits on the chest like a fat cat. Folk wake tireder than they laid down. The cooper hasn't coopered straight in a fortnight.

**The visions?**
Strangers. That's the wretched bit of it. I do not dream my own dreams anymore, sir, I dream the deaths of folk I never met. A drowned weaver one night, a burned shepherd the next, a boy with the bloody flux. I taste their last suppers. I feel them go cold. My own dreams, the ones I owned, they're crowded out, shoved aside like a paying guest by a deadbeat cousin.

**Remedies tried.**
All of them, sir, every fool one. Prayer to The Silver-Masked Sleeper, knee-bruised on the boards. Poppy enough to drop a draught-horse. Salt under the pillow, salt on the lintel, salt in my mouth. Nothing. Nothing. I'd drink lye if it'd buy me a quiet night.

**Payment.**
Square gold. Seven coins, milled, no shavings. I know what I'm asking and I know what you are, even if you'd prefer I didn't. I keep an honest house, sir, but an honest house don't run on prayer. Seven, and I'll lay them in your palm myself.

## 7. INTERVIEW (3) — Weirmoth, at the bar of the Hooded Pillow

**Trade.**
Roadman, mostly. Grave-carter when the season's wet and the dead won't keep. Mystic for hire on a slow week, but only the small jobs, mind, I don't claim more than the spear earns. A man who claims more than his spear earns winds up on the wrong end of one. Ha. Cheers.

**The spear.**
Ah, her. Saint-and-bishop story, that one, you'll like it. Belonged to a saint, supposedly, who lanced a poisoned bishop's throat clean of abscess and saved the diocese, or so the tale runs in the cups. Now, here's the part the priests leave off, she whistles, this spear, low in the haft, whenever sickness is near. Hear that? No? Lucky us. Lucky you.

**Sleep.**
Me? Sleep like a stone. Never dream, not once, not since I was a lad. Dreams are for folk with leisure, and I'm not a leisured man.

## 11. INTERVIEW (3) — Weirmoth, while the gas dissipates

**Last night.**
Aye, well. Poorly. Don't make a face at me, I know what I said at the bar. A man's allowed to dress up his sleep for a stranger, isn't he. Truth of it, I was up till the small hours staring at the rafters. Saw a fishwife drown four separate times. Counted her teeth on the third go.

**The canary.**
Mister Ribbon, this is. Aren't you, you scrap. Yes you are. Yes you are. He goes ahead of me down the bad holes, sir, sings till he doesn't. When he doesn't, I don't. Simple arrangement. He's had three names before this one and outlasted all of them, haven't you, my lad.

**Inside.**
Work.

## 20. INTERVIEW (3) — Weirmoth, suspicion rising across the brewing-bench

**The reagents.**
What're they for, then. That's my question to your question. You're pocketing jars I haven't been told about, and the cloth on the bench is laid out neater than a barber's, and you've not once said the words "for me" or "for us." So. What're they for.

**Prepared to witness death.**
Aye. Aye, I can witness. That part I signed for, I remember the shape of it. An anonymous death, you said, a stranger's, only the unhabituated can carry the memory through clean. I'm unhabituated. I'm your mule. Fine. Long as it's as you laid it out, scholar. Long as. No funny detours into anybody I've drunk with.

**Terms.**
Hells, the terms favor you. Don't pretend they don't. You set the cloth, you set the jars, you set who drinks and who doesn't. But I'm down here now, aren't I. Came this far on my own boots. In for a copper, in for the whole purse. Pour the bloody cup.

## 23. INTERVIEW (3) — Fellow Sourbell, between exchanged blows in the dining hall

**Who were you.**
Oh, oh, oh. Polite of you to ask, after all this time. I was a Fellow, sir, of the Red Pew, third cabal of the second bench, and don't pretend the names mean nothing to you, your face has gone the colour of curdled milk. Fellow Sourbell, that was me. Brewer of the lower decoctions, keeper of the seventh ledger, voted twice for the gas-plan and once against, and I'd take the once-against back if I had a tongue still wet enough to bargain with. We were eleven, did you know? Eleven Fellows on the upper bench. We dined here, at this very table, off plate that the village potter glazed for us in trade for a binding-charm against weevils. I had a chair. The third from the head, on the left. There's still a notch in the leg from where I kicked it once arguing about the price of quicklime, can you credit it, quicklime, I died over a quarrel about quicklime and the price of it, near enough. We thought ourselves lordly, sir. We thought our seal would hold. We thought the gas was for the rivals at the door and not for the fool sat with his soup spoon halfway to his mouth.

**Sealed by.**
By you. Yes. By you yourself, Fellow Liddick, don't twitch the eyebrow at me. I know that hand. I know the marble lie of it. You.

**What you want.**
Not vengeance, sir, oh no, I haven't the wind for vengeance, listen to me wheeze. Only this, only the small thing, only that the fate of my fellows be at last be shared, in good fellowship, by Fellow Liddick. Heh. Heh heh. Only fair. Only fair.
