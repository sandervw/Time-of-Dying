## A1: Negotiation Under Pressure

"Captain, the sum you have named is such as might be exacted of a pilgrim prince, and not of a humble scholar whose only freight is the dust of his own years; howbeit, I would not haggle in the manner of a fishmonger, for there are proprieties to be observed even upon the water."

"In lieu of your coin, I carry upon my person a periapt of no small antiquity, one which certain brothers of the lidless orders would account portentous, and which, were I at liberty to part with it, might more than balance your fee thrice over."

"Your reverence regards my satchel with what I must call a searching eye; I would entreat you to remember that a wayfarer's baggage is ofttimes burdened with inheritances whose provenance is obscure even unto the bearer."

"Captain, I would speak further in the cabin, ere the hour grows unseasonable and the current, which I am told is retrograde at this tide, compels an earlier departure than either of us had purposed."

"Be assured, my word is bond enow: the barge shall carry nothing aboard it that is not already mine to bestow."

## A2: Explaining Away a Disaster

"Good sirs, the conflagration, howsoever lamentable in its appearance, was no doing of mine in any compassable sense; the tinder of that stable was already in a condition so crepuscular and dry that a single errant spark, whether from lantern or from the flint of providence itself, would have served the same office as my own misfortune."

"I was passing through, as divers may attest, upon an errand of some urgency, and had I not bestirred myself to drag what remnants could be saved from the inferno, your surviving beast there, singed though he be, had gone the way of his fellows into the ash."

"For recompense, I would suggest that the matter be weighed against the service already rendered, and that the owner account his singed survivor a kind of weal from the flames, rather than dun a stranger for damages which, in strict equity, are the province of older negligence than mine."

## A3: Monologue While Alone

"So then: the friend is proved the traitor, and the city below, which I had been wont to name mine, is now a lagerstätte of my own pride, its bones and its bricks laid down in strata I may yet read if the light permits."

"Common opinion, were any here to voice it, would account me mazed; for what wight, being so comprehensively undone, stands upon his own ruined walls as if there were still some watch to be kept?"

"Yet I find I am not mazed, but only attentive, as a physician attends a patient whose etiology is at last become plain."

"Betrayal, withal, is no novel pestilence; it is endemic to the court, to the bedchamber, and to the table at which bread is broken, and I should have read its signs as readily as a mycologist reads the volva of a poisonous agaric."

"The dawn is cold, and the wind that comes up from the river smells of wet ash and of the Sweetmilk of the Well, which no man should drink."

"What shall I do, then? I shall descend. I shall not hie after the traitor in the mode of the wronged prince of old tragedies, for that satisfaction is for lesser wights."

"Rather shall I go down into the Chrypt beneath this tower, where certain of my ancestors keep their long obsequies, and I shall petition them in the deadspeech, for they have much yet to say concerning the husbandry of trust."

"And when I rise again, it shall be to a different accounting."

## B1: Rewrite Cugel's Con Artistry

"You would do well, sirrah, to moderate the intemperance of your address; for I am Othelmedir, and I stand here not as a petitioner but upon the express solicitation of Soldinck, whose instructions in this matter are neither ambiguous nor, I should think, unknown to you."

"My quarters, therefore, you will show me with such civility as the occasion demands, ere our mutual business grows further vexed."

"A light collation will suffice, as I broke my fast before the sun had yet risen above the eastern waters; nothing elaborate is required."

"Eminently so; the appointments are sufficient, and I shall be well accommodated here for the duration of the voyage."

"Soldinck has charged me with certain simple offices, to be undertaken as opportunity presents itself, the particulars whereof I shall make known to him who has need to know them, and to no other."

## B2: Rewrite Lord Gro's Philosophical Reflection

"Common opinion, I doubt not, shall account me mazed; for what man of sound judgement so rashly puts his life in hazard, and this in the very season when by patience and courage and such politic wisdom as I can muster I had wrested from fortune that which, hitherto, she had obstinately withheld?"

"Yet it is common opinion that is the fool, and not I; for he who imagines, after the long swink of his labours, to attain unto some lasting joy, might as lief beat water in a mortar, or essay to keep flame in a sieve."

"But because the dawn in her first hours so bewitched me, must I therefore love her still when, glutted with triumph, she settles to her garish noon? Nay; rather I turn, as now I turn, to Demonland in the sad sunset of her pride, and find there, withal, a kind of promise the noonday could not give."

## B3: Rewrite Othelmedir's Remote Instruction

"It is Othelmedir who speaks, sir."

"I petitioned certain of the dead, some of them rendered thus by the blade that rides upon your hip, and by their deadspeech I descried your whereabouts."

"This is a remote necromancy, and I have no seconds for chaff: within the reservoir before you, a reservoir at once mineral and spiritual, lies one scarab of particular interest to me, being of the substance of an ancestor who has languished overlong in the Stope of the Ninth Ozone, upon a white stone shelf, within a red scarab."

"Shatter that scarab, and all debts your Lady owes to me are thereby balanced, and the ledger closed."

"Moreover, you shall descry near that shelf a stone of peculiar aspect; a quite peculiar stone, withal, and a further reward to the expeditious."

"One parting recommendation: be betimes about it. There are too many lifetimes inside."

## C1: The Flooding of New Londo

"The great remedicians of New Londo, upon whose obsequies no kindly epitaph has yet been written, were brought at length to a choice of the hardest kind; for the Darkwraiths, whose nature I shall not here descant upon at length, were by then so multiplied within the city's lower precincts that no cordon of steel nor of deadspeech could any longer compass them."

"They chose, therefore, to sacrifice the city entire: the water locks were drawn shut, and the great sluices were opened, and New Londo, whose towers had stood since before the Time of Dying was yet named, was given over to the flood."

"The denizens, of whom some were kin to me and some were strangers and some were neither, drowned; and drowning, they were not released, but continue even now in spectral forms to swim those waters, the weft of their robes unknit, their mouths open in a keening that no living ear should descry."

"The remedicians themselves took up the keepership of the lock, and there they stand yet, in their long watch, lest some fool or some enemy essay to drain what is now the watery seal of that place."

"Withal, I must confess that the threat is only in part contained; for the Four Kings, and the Darkwraiths that attend upon them, receded not into oblivion but into the Abyss, by a covenant they had sworn, ere the deluge, with the primordial serpent whose name I will not speak at this table."

## C2: The Birth of the Chaos Flame

"To me, for my knowledge of matters astronomic and exequial, it falls to rehearse what was done at Izalith, and what was wrought there, and why; and I shall be plain, albeit the subject itself resists plainness."

"The Witch, whose domain was that great city set hard against the archtree swamp, and whose city stood nearer to the Abyss and to the advancing dark than any other, felt herself constrained to act; for the First Flame was dying, as all flames do, and she alone, among the bearers of a Lord Soul, had such mastery of the fire arts as might presume to its recreation."

"She had borne, by that time, seven daughters, and was then quick with her first male child; and in that condition, whether by hubris or by a necessity I shall not undertake to judge, she essayed the kindling anew."

"What followed was calamity beyond all common measure; for the flame she called forth, meeting her Lord Soul within the cradle of her working, took on a chaotic and ungovernable character, and tainted her utterly, and consumed her where she stood."

"She fled, so far as flight was yet hers, into the bole of an archtree, and there was delivered of her son, a creature disfigured past the recognition even of his own mother, whom the living now name Ceaseless Discharge, for his nature is to pour forth without surcease the contagion that begat him."

"The Chaos Flame engulfed mother and tree alike, and the three, which is to say woman and archtree and flame, were fused into one being, which is called the Bed of Chaos; and from that Bed, as from a noisome parturition, fires spring forth unquelled."

"And those fires tapped deep into the chaotic humours of Izalith's inhabitants, and of the swamp itself, and all save the strongest were altered; their flesh was taken from them, and their reason withal, and they became demons, and so remain, ere any remedy that I have heard of can compass their recall."
