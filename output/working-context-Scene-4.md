# Scene 4 Context

## Premise

POV: Limited 3rd-person (Othelmedir) | Tense: Past | Wordcount: 1,100

Othelmedir, a necromancer returning under a false identity, has hired a sellsword named Weirmoth to help him destroy the cauldron beneath the Tower of the Attercop -- a device he himself built, which is leaking necromantic gas into a nearby village and replacing the living residents' memories with the death-visions of the preserved dead inside. Both the tower's original cabal (The Red Pew) and the invaders who breached it (Milkmoot) are preserved in the gas and now begin to rouse as Othelmedir and Weirmoth enter.

## Current Scene Outline

### 4. The Waking Halls
**Wordcount:** 1,100

Outer chambers. Old invaders, long-preserved in the gas, rouse and attack. **Vicious, excrutiatingly-specific fighting** takes place between the two reanimating cabals of necromancers (invaders and tower residents), and Othelmedir/Weirmoth - describe specific maneuvers, movements, numbers of foes, how bodies are affected. Weirmoth notes how Othelmedir seems to know every corridor. They encounter a "muscle-wall" a barrier of flesh woven over the passage to the lower sanctums of the tower; preventing the further release of the gas, and further progress.
- Gas pools knee-deep in low corners, pale green, smelling of spoiled meat
- Every surface wears a velvet patina of spider silk; footsteps leave dark bruises
- Dead, awakening invaders still gripping the weapons; mouths work silently, reciting specific, spoken-dialogue breach-orders.
- A lectern where three common books sit open to the same page, spines cracked: 'On the Conduct of Sealed Houses', 'Compendium of Threshold Speech', and 'A Practitioner's Almanac'
- A low sweet rot
- A preserved ledger nailed to a column, names beside duty-hours, most hours long past
- One invader wears a cabalistic pendant (Hammered tin; sigil of a lidded cauldron with a single open eye on the lid; shape of a flat tear); Othelmedir pockets it
- (Othelmedir) Internal line appraising which tissues on the corpses survived the gas best
- (Othelmedir) Never reaches for a weapon; Weirmoth does the killing
- Othelmedir straightens a crooked wall sconce without looking
- (Weirmoth) Competent in the fight, spear-work is clean and unflashy; but lost in the chrypt arcana, defers to Othelmedir
- Weirmoth taps his canary, but it says nothing
- (lore) A rival mancer, Ostrobone the Rook, led the breach wearing a mask of hornet husks

## Necessary Backward/Forward Context

- Othelmedir unsealed the door himself, and did so with easy familiarity -- Weirmoth has already noticed he carries no weapon and is beginning to register that Othelmedir moves with knowledge that exceeds a wandering scholar's. This unease should quietly escalate: Weirmoth notices Othelmedir knows every corridor.
- (Withheld for this scene) The dead -- specifically the Red Pew members -- murmur an old name when they sense Othelmedir. The composer must write around this: the mouths move silently or the words are indistinct, but something about Othelmedir's presence agitates the tower dead specifically. Do not reveal the name "Fellow Liddick" here.
- (Withheld, carried forward) Weirmoth was once a seminarian of a minor road-cult; his shroud-cloak is his old vestment; Mister Ribbon was his augury bird. This is never stated in this scene -- but it explains why Weirmoth watched the unsealing deadspeech unflinching in Scene 3 ("the cadence is almost familiar to him"), and why he taps the canary for a sign here and gets nothing.
- The pendant Othelmedir pockets (hammered tin, lidded-cauldron sigil, single open eye on the lid, flat-tear shape) is a Milkmoot cabalistic marker. It reappears in Scene 9 -- Othelmedir keeps it even after yielding his stolen reagents, because Weirmoth's bargain never named it.
- Ostrobone the Rook is named here for the first time (lore drop, not a character who appears physically in this scene).
- The muscle-wall encountered at the scene's end is the obstacle that drives the next three scenes (Scriptorium, Still-Room, and the dream-potion arc). It must be described as genuinely impassable by conventional means, blocking the passage to the lower sanctums.
