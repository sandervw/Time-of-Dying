# Dreamreel — Verse Blobs

## 5. VERSE

At first cockcrow, with the bluff still grey beneath us
we climbed by the goat-track to the door I had not touched in twenty winters
and there found the lintel curtained over with attercop silk
each strand thick as harp-string
each strand greasy with the slow dust of decades

...

I laid my palm upon the Seal of Neinstitch
and gave my whispered deadspeech to its sutures, low and patient
naming the binding by its true name
which the rival cabal had thought hidden from any tongue but theirs

...

The sutures parted with a sigh, as of an old man unbuckling
and from the breach there vented a pale gas, sallow and slow
the colour of a sick mare's tooth
and weighted, as such vapors are, to the floor

...

Therefore stepped I back to a clean elevation of the path
and Weirmoth with me, his spear-haft braced
and we waited, watchful, while the green tide thinned against the threshold
until the sill ran clear and the doorway stood open as a yawning mouth

## 8. VERSE

In the first chamber the invaders roused themselves at our intrusion
seven of them, mail rusted into the meat beneath
their helms grown into their skulls by a slow corruption of iron and bone
and they came forward with the lurching gait of forgotten purpose

...

Their mouths worked at the old breach-orders, silent as fish
shaping the wrong words at the wrong target
for the gas had taken their breath three hundred seasons gone
and yet the discipline of the cabal held them to their posts

...

Weirmoth's spear entered a throat where the mail and the meat had grown one
and the point came out clean, the body buckling without protest
and another stepped into the gap in their line
and another after that, faithful to a captain long since rendered to silt

...

I attended in the meantime to a sconce gone crooked upon the wall
straightening the iron as one might right a lamp at prayer
and from the neck of a pierced cultist I lifted a hammered tin pendant
and slipped it, unremarked, into the inner pocket of my robe

## 10. VERSE

The lower passage gave us no passage at all
for the corridor had been stoppered by a wall of muscle
sinew laid crosswise upon sinew
flesh woven through the doorframe like the warp and weft of some butcher's loom

...

Weirmoth set the butt of his spear to it, a tentative tap
and the wall returned a wet drum-thump from its inner chambers
and twitched, once, all along its surface
like the flank of a dreaming horse

...

He swore then, low and Welsh in his throat
while I stood at a scholar's distance and read the meat as one reads a recipe
noting the gristle, the suture, the slow industry of its making
and the manner in which it had been grown rather than built

...

I named what was wanted
a poison brewed to eleven reagents, no fewer
the formula of which I had set down in my own hand long before this trespass
and so we turned aside, by the side-stair, for the still-room above

## 14. VERSE

The still-room had its warden, as I had thought it must
a dead-dreamer eyeless in the candle-dark
its sockets stitched, its fingers sunk past the second knuckle in the cold kettle
moving them slowly, as one stirs a porridge that has gone to skin

...

Weirmoth gave it the spear without ceremony
the thrust clean beneath the ribs and out through the spine
and the thing folded down upon itself without a sound
its hands sliding, last of all, from the brass

...

He wiped the haft on the curtain at the alembic, twice
and pretended at the casual labor of a man who has done such work often
though I marked the small tremor at his jaw
and kept my own counsel concerning it

...

Thereafter I set the still upon its trivet
and laid out the eleven reagents in the order of their introduction
measuring each by the antique weights I had carried up from the boarding house
and beginning the brew in the silence proper to such work

## 17. VERSE

The breach potion, when poured, ate the muscle-wall to threads
the meat dissolving in slow gouts of pale liquor
the sinew unbraiding itself like rotted rope
until what had been a stoppered throat stood open as a confessional

...

Then began our descent, and with it the rising of the outer cabal
the watchers and the cantors and the lay-brothers, their robes patinaed in silk
and Weirmoth's spear kept its clean rhythm at my shoulder
one stroke, one pause, one stroke, the cadence of a roof-beater at thatch

...

We passed the dining hall as we went
where the bread sat black upon the trenchers
and the candle-stubs leaned in their sconces like drunken porters
and the chairs lay upon their backs as the cabal had left them in their last alarm

...

Beyond it lay the ritual circle of the Red Pew, my circle
the chalk-lines vivid still upon the slate
the cardinal sigils unsmudged by any foot in twenty winters
and I did not pause, though my eye lingered the breadth of one cautious breath

## 18. VERSE

Fellow Sourbell rose then from the alcove on our left
his robe gone to lace beneath the silk
and from a throat that had not breathed since the year of the breach
he spoke a single name, soft as a man speaks in sleep
and the name was Liddick

...

Fellow Alder the Lank rose answering at the alcove opposite
his tongue dry as the old paper of an unread psalter
and he repeated the name after his colleague
once, and then again
the syllables falling between us like coins upon a slab

...

Weirmoth glanced at me, the briefest cut of his eye
and I gave him nothing in return, no flicker, no syllable, no sign
and he set his spear through Sourbell at the hollow of the breastbone
and the Fellow folded, still murmuring the name into the floor

...

Together we took Alder down between us
my hand upon the haft beside Weirmoth's
and the name kept echoing in the corridor after the body had stilled
a small voice, behind us and beside us, naming what I had thought long buried

## 20. VERSE

Thus came we at last to the Cauldron Chamber at the tower's foot
where the great vessel sat stoppered upon its iron trivet
and I uncorked the stopper by the eleven turns proper to it
and poured the Lidd Decoction in a slow line down the inner wall

...

The contents hissed at the meeting
and climbed in a muddied froth the height of two hands above the rim
and stood there a breath, considering
and then collapsed, all at once, to a flat and lightless muck

...

I intoned the closing deadspeech over the muck for the better part of an hour
naming each binding and each unbinding by their archaic appellations
until the cauldron sat inert beneath my voice
and the gas in the air thinned to nothing more than the smell of old clay

...

And turning then from the cauldron, well-pleased with the labor of the morning
I found Weirmoth standing at three paces upon my left
his spear no longer at his shoulder
but lowered to the low guard, the point steady, the knuckles pale upon the haft
