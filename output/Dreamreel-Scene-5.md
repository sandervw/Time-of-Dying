# Dreamreel: Scene 5

The door opened onto grey, not darkness — the false-sun globe suspended above the central gallery having long since emptied of whatever luminance it once held, its dead face casting light that was not so much light as the negation of shadow, flat and without warmth or direction, so that Othelmedir's boot found the first flagstone's raised corner before his eyes had adjusted. He stumbled, yet straightened without comment and without breaking stride, his second foot already correcting for the descent his first had not anticipated, the hand at his side dropping to the rail without his needing to look for it.

Weirmoth moved along the balcony rail, one gloved hand running along the stone — cold even through the leather, the drop to the dead garden below shifting in and out of view through flat pillars as he walked — while Othelmedir examined the shelves. His hand moved from spine to spine and then stopped, the third shelf from the floor, second column from the corner, as though the shelf had been waiting for it. The spine gave under his fingers, soft and yielding, its cover having absorbed so many seasons of damp that it had become a texture rather than a structure, and he drew it carefully free of its neighbors. The volume was heavier than one would expect from its mold-thinned cover. He opened it to a page at the middle, and the ink, at least, had been preserved.

A sour taste — given off by the ankle-high gas — suffused the library; something at the edge of rancid, which settled at the back of the throat and lingered there as if it had business with the soft palate.

"That one worth reading?" Weirmoth had stopped at the rail, watching him.

"It is a treatise, Weirmoth," Othelmedir said, though he did not close the book. "Which is to say it repays study rather than light perusal, and this particular treatise — *Potions and Poisons of Eleven Reagents* — would repay more study than most of the volumes which have kept it company."

He turned a page, and another. His thumb found a margin and lingered there. The garden below, with its small humped islands of pale grassless soil rising from the ankle-deep sea of gas, each island marking where the cabal had buried one of its mancers, receded; the rail receded; Weirmoth receded. There remained the page, and his thumb on the margin, and the slow rising suspicion — though suspicion was not the word for it, nor anything so tangible as a thought — that the shelves around him contained matter for which the village above, and the man behind him, and the coin in the man's purse, were not adequate weights to set upon the other pan.

"The gas," said Weirmoth. "You were going to tell me how it works."

"The gas, Weirmoth, yes." Othelmedir closed the book with care, finger marking his place. "The cauldron in this tower is stoppered, yet elements seep regardless, as a sealed vessel of brine will still sweat salt through its seams; and those elements dispersing outward are what produce the Dreamreel. The deaths the villagers have been dreaming are not visions but residue."

He moved to a short spiral stair, and Weirmoth followed. They descended to the lower floor and turned to regard a door half-hidden behind a shelf — a door which Othelmedir, walking past it, did not look at directly, and yet whose threshold his shoulders had already squared themselves against.

A sound came through the wood before he had finished turning: low, sustained, the sound of something drawing breath after a long stillness, the drawing-in of a long-stoppered vessel. Othelmedir's hand tightened on the treatise's spine.

Weirmoth had drawn back a half-step into the open space below the dead sun, between the garden plots, his weight settling. "That ain't the wind."

"It is not the wind, Weirmoth, no." Othelmedir tucked the treatise beneath his arm and regarded the sellsword with an expression he hoped was unreadable. "Your metier, I think."
