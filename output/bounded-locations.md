# Bounded Locations

## The Lain Belfry

A pilgrim-tower that toppled intact onto its side along a ridge above Hornwater. The original eight bells now hang inside it as a row of teardrop chambers, each large enough to walk through. Pilgrims pass bell to bell along the prone shaft. Each chamber rings at a different fundamental when struck from inside.

**Why it earns story space:** Eight bell-chambers in a row give you a natural episodic frame: one story per bell, each chamber's fundamental coloring the tale told inside it.

## Coldfloor

A village built atop a small Meathook offshoot deposit of frozen undead. Cottages are floored in unbroken blue ice. Hearths stand on stone platforms raised eighteen inches above the ice so the floor doesn't melt. Stoop down, and you can see a face beneath your kitchen.

**Why it earns story space:** A whole village's worth of small domestic stories, all unified by one strange physical fact (the face under your kitchen floor) that any vignette can pivot off.

## The Polyp Dome

A side-branch off Mellowdepth, the undersea marble pipe carrying the Road. The branch terminates in a glass cupola set flush with the seafloor. A dozen dwellers live in carved alcoves along the pipe-wall, lit by polyp luminescence, eating shellfish that crawl in through brass grates.

**Why it earns story space:** A dozen named alcove-dwellers in a seafloor pipe-end. Cast-sized, claustrophobic, and the only way in or out is a long marble tube. Whatever happens happens *here.*

## The Shingleroof

A two-story manor whose roof is tiled, not in slate, but in family epitaphs laid edge-on in overlapping courses. Rainwater drains through inscribed grooves. The interior is unnaturally muffled, as if the air upstairs has been pressed flat by all that interment-grade stone overhead.

**Why it earns story space:** Domestic in scale, a single family's manor. The uncanny premise (interment-grade silence pressing down from overhead) is intimate enough for one tight short, and quiet enough not to overwhelm character work.
