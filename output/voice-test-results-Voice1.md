## A1: Negotiation Under Pressure

"And yet, ere you name so grievous a sum for so brief a passage, consider well that I come not as some pelf-hungry traveler but as one whose services in the domain of exequial obligation are, as certain parties among the riverborn will attest, of considerable and compounding worth."

"There are those who carry coin and those who carry rarer goods, and what reposes within my traveling satchel is of a provenance so singular, and of an antiquity so considerable, that a man of discernment would weigh it against your price and find the balance tipped in my favor."

"I perceive that our friend in the grey vestments watches me with some disposition toward suspicion; howbeit, it is perhaps he, not I, whose interest in this article would merit scrutiny, were the matter to be opened before witnesses."

"Name a figure befitting the crossing of a hired vessel, not the ransom of a burial-king, and I will consent to say no more of what I carry or whence it came."

## A2: Explaining Away a Disaster

"It would be, I submit, an error of considerable consequence to lay this matter at my feet without first conducting so much as a cursory inquiry into the conditions that preceded it, for there are those among the stable-hands who, were they questioned with even moderate diligence, would confirm that the straw in question was of a ruinously dry and volatile quality, and had been arranged with no great attention to the proximity of the lamp."

"The horse that survives, and whose condition I note with professional sympathy, is not without its own peculiar fortune, for the singed coat of a beast that has walked so near to conflagration is, in certain traditions of alchemical husbandry, regarded as a mark of no inconsiderable durability."

"I will not insult your intelligence by suggesting the matter is trivial, and yet I would ask you, before you reach any determination of culpability, to weigh the question of whether a scholar of my standing and preoccupation would waste so much as a moment's effort on the burning of straw; the fire, I submit, had its own originating causes, of which I was merely an unfortunate and proximate observer."

## A3: Monologue While Alone

"And so it is come to this, and at this hour, and beneath a sky still livid at its eastern hem, where the light crawls up as though reluctant to illuminate what it must."

"I have stood upon these battlements before in a posture of dominion, when all that lay below was mine in deed and in the sworn covenant of those who served beneath me, and the stones of that city carried my ordinance in their mortar, and the dead within it were duly registered and catalogued and obedient to my custodianship."

"It is, I confess, a matter of no small interest to me that betrayal, which the common run of scholars describe as a sudden and catastrophic event, arrives not so but by slow accretion, and that the very proofs I now recall were present to me and were by me, in my habitual generosity of interpretation, reframed as loyalty deferred."

"The one whom I trusted above the others, and whose knowledge of my methods and my pacts and my stored obligations was natheless comprehensive enough to wound me in every quarter at once, has chosen this season to act; and I find, somewhat to my own surprise, that I am not disordered by it."

"What I feel is perhaps the cold and clarifying sensation that the taphonomist names algor mortis: the body's heat departing, and the tissues contracting into a new and harder arrangement."

"There are no gods remaining who would receive a petition, and the dead of this city, whose bones lie under stone and rubble in their several charnel ranks, cannot counsel me; and yet it is they, not the living, who have always been my most reliable constituents."

"I shall descend from here and I shall attend to certain preparations, and before the sun has reached the cusp of its meridian there will be pacts made and obligations set in motion, and the one who has chosen to act against me will find that the deadspeech does not forgive a broken covenant, not in this life and not in the long tenure of the one that follows."

## B1: Rewrite Cugel's Con Artistry

"I would counsel you, and counsel you sincerely, to moderate both the pitch and the presumption of your address, for I am one who has come at the express solicitation of Soldinck himself, whose name I trust carries within this household the appropriate weight, and whose invitation it would be, I suspect, a matter of some professional consequence for you to have seen dishonored."

"You may show me to such quarters as have been prepared, and the civility of your manner in doing so will be taken, I assure you, as a point in your favor when I make my report."

"A light collation would be most welcome, as the hour of my breaking fast was early and the road demanding; I will trouble you no further than that."

"I am, all things considered, well-disposed toward the arrangements here, and Soldinck's commission, which requires of me a number of tasks of no exceptional complexity during the course of the voyage, is the sole reason for my presence."

## B2: Rewrite Lord Gro's Philosophical Reflection

"It is given to common opinion to account me reckless and to name this season of peril the one I chose for folly, and yet I have labored long and with a patience not easily sustained, and by politic wisdom of no inconsiderable degree, and through the endurance of what fortune, that most froward of dispensers, had withheld from me through many a travail, to arrive at what I have now obtained."

"Natheless common opinion is not my confessor; he who labors under the persuasion that great effort culminates in lasting joy is as one who beats water in a mortar and catalogues the froth as progress, and in this the common opinion is the greater fool."

"And yet because the day at its dawning hour bewitched me with a particular and pellucid glamour, it does not follow that I am obliged to love it when, glutted with its own meridian triumph, it descends into the garish and the commonplace; and so it is that I turn, as I turn now, toward that which is yet declining and yet proud, toward Demonland in the sad sunset of her glory, wherein there is more of what I seek than in any burning noon."

## B3: Rewrite Othelmedir's Remote Instruction

"It is Othelmedir, and you will know me by the quality of the silence attending this communication, which is not the silence of distance but of precision."

"I petitioned, as is my custom, certain of the dead in whose company I hold standing, and among those whose tongues I unlocked were some whose passage into that condition was, I have reason to believe, expedited by the blade you carry at your hip; they were, howbeit, obliging in their counsel, and they directed me to you with admirable economy."

"What you are now experiencing is a remote conveyance of deadspeech, and because such workings carry their charge in finite measure, I will not spend it on preamble: within the reservoir you will be shown, in that mineral and spiritual architecture known as the Stope of the Ninth Ozone, upon a shelf of white stone, reposes a red scarab, and within that scarab is preserved an ancestor of mine who has, for reasons I need not detail to you, languished there overlong."

"Shatter the scarab, and the full sum of your Lady's debts to me, which are in their aggregate considerable, shall be accounted balanced and discharged and of no further material consequence between us."

"You will notice, in proximity to that shelf, a stone of a character rather singular and compelling; this too is a reward, and it is yours, and I suggest you observe it with care."

"One recommendation before this working closes: be expeditious in the execution of what I have described, for the scarab contains, within its cortège of compressed soul-matter, rather more accumulated lifetimes than is comfortable to stand near for any extended duration."

## C1: The Flooding of New Londo

"I was present, as one of perhaps a dozen witnesses whose continued existence the great remedicians deemed insufficiently consequential to suppress, and I can report what followed with a particularity that I do not think the historical record will elsewhere provide."

"The choice that confronted those learned practitioners was not, as it has since been described by those who were not there, a choice between sacrifice and inaction, but between two forms of catastrophe, and what they elected, after deliberation of a brevity that I still find troubling, was to close the water locks and to flood the city entire, with its full population and its accumulated obligations and its living throats."

"The denizens of New Londo drowned in an order that I will not describe in detail, for the detail is available to me and I find no benefit in its repetition; what endured of them afterward were the spectral forms, which do not rest but swim, still, in that submerged and sepulchral dark."

"The remedicians remain above the lock as its wardens, and their covenant is with the seal itself, and any who approach with the intention of draining that accumulated burial-water are turned away or otherwise discouraged."

"And yet the containment is not, and was not from the first, entire; the Four Kings and those who served them in their Darkwraith capacity receded not by the will of the remedicians but by virtue of a pact with one of the primordial serpents, whose interest in such arrangements is, it would seem, coeval with the world's first darkening, and who cannot be accounted among the forces of remedy."

## C2: The Birth of the Chaos Flame

"What I am about to recount occurred before this present reckoning of years, in the great city of Izalith, which occupied the territory adjacent to the archtree swamp and stood, of all the old cities, nearest to the abyss in whose advancing margin the primordial dark was gathering force."

"The Witch of Izalith, in whom the mastery of fire-arts was, by all scholarly attestation, more perfectly housed than in any other living practitioner, and who held moreover one of the Lord Souls in her sovereign custody, perceived the failing of the first flame and was moved by a compulsion which I am not qualified to characterize as either wisdom or hubris, though I note that the outcome may furnish some evidence on the question."

"She had borne seven daughters, and was at the time of her experiment great with her first male child; natheless she undertook to recreate the first flame, and the result was a conflagration not of illumination but of chaos, wherein the Lord Soul and the new flame combined into a thing uncontrollable and contaminating."

"She was consumed by it, and sought refuge within an archtree, and it was there, in that refuge which was already no refuge, that she was delivered of her son, a being horribly disfigured and bearing in his very flesh the taint of the Chaos Flame, whom those who named him called Ceaseless Discharge, which name is, I submit, more diagnostic than any iatric catalogue."

"The flame engulfed her and the archtree together, and the three of them, mother and son and fire, were fused into a new and singular entity called the Bed of Chaos, from which uncontrollable conflagrations issued forth and spread into the swamp and into the city itself, and the residents of Izalith, in whose constitutions some chaotic susceptibility had apparently been seeded, were tainted and transformed into demons, all save those whose inner fortification was sufficient to resist; and the swamp, too, was remade in the image of that disorder, and has not since recovered."
