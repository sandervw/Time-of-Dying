# The Song You Carried In: Scene 4

## Full Scene

The passage dropped. The walls closed in.

Igon descended sideways where the stone pinched too tight for his shoulders. His pauldrons scraped both walls and ground sandstone to a fine grit that hung in the still air and would not settle. Each breath roared inside his helm. The visor slits fogged with each exhale and cleared, fogged and cleared, so the passage ahead strobed between blur and dim. He breathed through his nose. The sound of it filled the iron shell of his head like wind in a bottle.

No dead here. The passage was too narrow for anything but rats and men, and no rats lived this deep.

His Hip Light swung at his belt, a fist-sized jar of cold phosphor on a short chain, and threw a weak yellow circle across the stone. In that light he saw them: thin channels cut into both walls at shoulder height, running in parallel grooves toward the dark below. Pipes. He stopped and pressed his gauntlet to one. The iron hummed back. It was warm.

He pulled his hand away.

He had hummed descending. He had sung to keep his nerves from fraying. Every note he made had been drawn through these channels and poured into corpses like breath into a bellows. The dead had not risen because he fought them. They had risen because he opened his mouth. He stood with his back against the far wall and breathed through his teeth and said nothing.

The dust motes drifted in the lamp's beam, suspended. Something below held them. A thin whistle came from behind, the passage pulling air past him, and the sustained note from the deep climbed through his boot soles and settled in his jaw.

The passage ended at a double door. Runes cut deep into the wood, filled with black wax that smelled of tallow and cold iron. Necromancer work. Someone had sealed this place and left the song to rot inside it.

Igon drew his Blue Glass Claymore, its pale edge cold in the lamplight, and drove it through the seal. The wax cracked. The wood splintered. He stepped through.
