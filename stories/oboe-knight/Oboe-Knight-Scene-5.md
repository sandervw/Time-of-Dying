# The Song You Carried In: Scene 5

## Full Scene

The vestibule opened wide. Igon stood at the threshold and said nothing. His hip-lamp, the flat iron lantern bolted to his left pauldron, threw its cold blue light against the far wall.

Twelve alcoves lined the walls, six to a side, and in each alcove a corpse sat upright on a stone bench. They wore silver cloth sewn with garnets and pale sapphires. The thread-joins had tarnished green. Hip-light caught the jewels and threw colored flecks across the flagstone like scattered dice.

He stepped in. His boots rang on stone, and the sound carried. Pipes threaded the walls here too, thicker than those above, their mouths wide as fists. A resonance chamber. He understood that now. Everything built to amplify. Everything built to harvest.

The sustained chord he had tracked since the upper chambers was louder here, pressing against the stone. He could feel it in his teeth. He counted the alcoves the way a man counts stairs in the dark.

Igon crossed to the center of the vestibule. Twelve dead in silver.

The first corpse turned its head. Silver cloth tore at the collar like ripped parchment. Then the second. Then all of them at once, twelve heads swiveling, twelve mouths falling open with a sound like dry wood splitting.

They sang. Twelve mouths at once, and the note that issued was Igon's own voice.

He knew it. The melody he had hummed at the crystal door, the tune he had carried since childhood, now poured from twelve dead throats in a chord that filled the vestibule like warm light. And it was beautiful.

The nearest corpse lurched from its alcove. Igon split it from collarbone to hip with the Blue Glass Claymore, a longsword ground from a single slab of grave-quarried glass, its edge never once repaired. The blade caught hip-light and threw a wet blue arc across the ceiling. Garnets and sapphires scattered from the ruined silver cloth and bounced across the flagstone like bright teeth. The corpse fell in two halves. The choir did not falter. Eleven voices carried the note higher.

"What does it feel like," said the second corpse. Its hood slipped back. The face beneath still held skin, thin as onion peel, translucent over brown bone. It spoke in a flat, musical tone, clinical, curious. "The warmth. In the chest. What does it feel like."

Igon took its head. He did not answer.

Three came at him from the left alcoves. He planted his back foot and swung in a wide lateral cut that sheared through two at the ribs and knocked the third sideways into the wall. Tohmb dust erupted in amber clouds, glowing in the hip-light. His breathing hammered inside the helm, fogging the visor slits. Sweat pooled in his gauntlets. The claymore hilt slipped a quarter-turn in his grip and he tightened his fist.

"Fascinating," sang a corpse from behind him. He wheeled and drove the blade through its sternum. "You tighten when you strike. The song brightens. Do you feel it brighten."

"Be quiet," said Igon.

His voice rang through the helm, warm and full, and the pipes in the walls drank it. The chord swelled. He heard it happen. He knew what it meant.

Four more lurched from the far alcoves. The singing bored through his helm and he could not hold his tongue against it.

"Come on then," he said, swinging. "I have walked further than this. I have fought worse than you." Throat, chest, chest, skull. Each word louder than the last. The choir thickened with every syllable, his own warm voice feeding back through dead mouths until the vestibule rang like a struck bell.

"You are less now," sang the last three in unison. "Can you hear it. You were more when you entered."

"I can hear my own song," said Igon. "But that... is not the song in... my dreams..."

The last three he destroyed in two strokes. The vestibule fell silent.

Then the pipes in the walls began to hum. Visibly. Dust vibrated off their rims and drifted in the hip-light. The chord rebuilt itself without any throat to carry it, rising from the stone itself, from the pipes, from the jewels ground into the flagstone, from every surface his song had touched.

It was his melody and his voice, whole and complete, louder and richer than he had ever sung it. He stood at its center. The Blue Glass Claymore shed motes of corpse dust in the quiet. His song filled the room, and he was empty.

Beyond the vestibule, an archway opened into darkness. Something waited there, not physical, but like a question not answered. Igon only limped forward. He had nothing left to say.
