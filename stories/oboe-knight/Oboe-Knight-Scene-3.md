# The Song You Carried In: Scene 3

## Full Scene

The first chamber held four shrines. Igon touched the nearest offering bowl and the dried goods crumbled under his gauntlet, releasing a faint sweetness like old fruit leather. He lifted his fingers and sniffed. Someone had left apricots here, once. Plums, perhaps. A thousand years of mineral air had turned them to powder. Before the fourth shrine lay a carpet of woven black wool, matted with crystallized residue. He stepped wide.

"I have been looking for you," he said aloud, and his voice filled the chamber and rang against the sandstone. He was speaking to the tohmb. To whatever had made the sound that had followed him since childhood. "I heard an oboe on the wind when I was seven years old. It played for three days. No one else heard it. I have come a very long way."

The tohmb said nothing. He moved to the next shrine and found a knot of human hair bound with copper wire, sitting in its bowl like a small dead animal. The air was still. His glowbug lantern swung at his hip and threw his shadow in long arcs across the walls.

Something clicked behind him. Bone on stone. He turned.

A skeleton stood in the doorway of the second chamber. Its skull was cocked to one side, listening. It did not charge. It drifted toward him in a slow arc, its bone feet clicking on the stone like dice thrown on a table.

He drew the claymore. Blue glass caught the glowbug light and threw fractured reflections across the sandstone, and the skeleton turned its empty sockets toward the shimmer. A second rose behind it from a stone alcove, joints grinding, jaw working without sound.

Igon cut the first across the collarbone and it came apart like dry kindling. The second lunged. He split it at the pelvis with a rising stroke, and bone fragments skidded across the floor. He was breathing hard inside his helm and the visor fogged with each exhalation, the condensation beading and running in thin lines down the iron.

He moved through the chambers. At each shrine he stopped and tried a prayer. Palms upward for the Thin Man. Crossed wrists for the Scratcher. Thumb pressed to his lips for the Mother of Worms. Cupped hands for Gallbladder. Nothing answered. No warmth, no pull, no recognition. Whatever consecrated this place had done so long before the necromancer-gods were born.

In the third chamber he found a jaw-lyre among the grave goods: a stringed instrument built from a human mandible, the gut strings still taut after centuries. He did not touch it. A third skeleton rose from the floor behind the shrine and he backed toward the doorway, drawing it after him.

He gave ground deliberately, pulling the skeleton onto the black carpet.

The wool flashed white. The skeleton seized and cracked apart from the inside, every bone splitting lengthwise as though pressure had built within the marrow for centuries. Fragments struck the walls and ceiling. The carpet dimmed and lay still.

Three more stood in the passage beyond, swaying, their skulls turned toward him.

"None of that," said Igon.

He went through them at speed. One stroke apiece. Clean separation. The claymore rang against stone where the last one fell.

Silence. Then, from deeper in the tohmb, a sustained note rose. Louder now than it had been in the shrine room. Louder than in the passage. It had fed on the noise of each destroyed corpse, the crack of bone and the ring of iron, and grown fat on the sound.

Igon crossed to the main passage door. Iron, heavy, carved with a necromancer rune filled with black wax. He laid his gauntlet against it. The wax was faintly warm. He pushed, and struck the door with the claymore's pommel when it held, and the rune flared once, cold and blue, and the door did not move.

He found the crack in the adjacent wall where the sandstone had crumbled and left a jagged aperture barely wider than his shoulders. An armored man, chasing a sound from his childhood, squeezing through a gap in a rock. He turned sideways and pushed through. His iron plate scraped against the broken stone and threw sparks into the dark. He did not hum. He did not speak. The note below waited for him, patient and sustained.
