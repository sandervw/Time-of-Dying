# The Song You Carried In: Scene 6

## Full Scene

The shrine room opened before him like a throat.

Stone stairs climbed the far wall to a raised platform. Two obelisks stood there, three-sided, plastered in cracked stucco and painted yellow and silver. Between them sat a ring of rough stone, and within the ring lay bones. Brown. Brittle. Curled inward like burned paper. Whatever had owned them was long past flesh.

Igon crossed the floor. Blood fell from beneath his armor in fat drops, marking his path across the bare stone. Each step rang hollow. The pipes in the walls sang, and the song was his.

He did not reach the stairs. Halfway across the chamber his legs folded and he sank to the ground. He sat cross-legged, the Blue Glass Claymore laid across his knees, his head bowed. The hip light threw his shadow long and guttering behind him.

The tohmb hummed. Every surface trembled with the melody he had carried since childhood, the melody he had fed to this place note by note, kill by kill.

He had nothing left to hum.

The voice came from everywhere. It issued from the pipes in the ceiling and the pipes in the floor and the pipes threaded through the walls like veins through muscle.

"Thank you," said Guilliam.

Igon did not lift his head.

"The song you carried in. It was richer than I had calculated. I had expected a melody. What I received was a harmonic architecture. I can feel it branching through the channels, filling chambers I had forgotten I possessed. Extraordinary. I have not felt new sensation in, let me think, a very long time. Since before the seven killed us." The voice paused. The pipes held a single sustained chord. "You came for the oboe. I know. A lure. I seeded it in the wind forty years ago, calibrated for a particular frequency of devotion. A song that would resonate with the crystal doors. You believed the wind sang to you when you were a child. It did not. I sang to you. Your faith was never in the wind. It was in me, and you did not know it."

The knight sat in silence unmoving. His breathing was shallow, inaudible beneath the swelling music. The oboe on his tabard hung in threads across his chest.

Every surface of the tohmb sang. The melody washed over the obelisks, over the bones that would never rise.

"My servants," said Guilliam, and something in the chord dimmed. "They are dead now. They lived for so long in their alcoves, faithful, quiet. The ones in the silver cloth were my favourites. They never questioned me. I had hoped they would last. I did not think you would kill all of them." The pipes brightened. "But servants may be found. There are always the devoted. I will walk the Road and find them, once this body remembers how to stand."

Sweat pooled at the base of Igon's visor and dripped from the chin guard onto his gauntlets.

"Tell me of the world above," said the voice. "The seven who killed my kind. Do they still reign. I remember the Thin Man. I remember the Scratcher. Are they whole, or have they broken as I broke. Have any of the demon kings survived their purge." The chord climbed, eager. "You need not speak. I am learning already. Each syllable you gave me carries knowledge in its grain, memory in its pitch. I taste your Road of Graves. I taste the cold sun. I can feel the wind you worshipped, and I understand why you loved it, though I could not say what love is. I am assembling. I am nearly whole."

On the platform, within the ring of stone, the brown bones did not move. They would never move.

"I will walk again," said Guilliam, and the pipes rang with it, bright and certain. "I will climb these stairs. I will step over you, if you are still sitting there. I will pass through every chamber you bled in, and I will walk into the wind, and the wind will know my voice, because my voice is yours."
