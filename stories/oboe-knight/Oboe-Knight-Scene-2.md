# The Song You Carried In: Scene 2

## Full Scene

The chamber bent to the left. Igon swept the claymore in a slow arc before him and stepped through.

Shrines lined both walls, three to a side, cut from the same pale sandstone as the passage above. Each held an offering bowl. He crossed to the nearest and ran a gauntleted finger through the dust inside. Grit, mineral and dry. The residue of something placed here centuries ago and left to powder.

"Pilgrims," said Igon to no one. "Even down here."

The second bowl held a knotted cord and a clay thumb-ring, and the cord was tied in a pattern he didn't recognize, seven knots at intervals that weren't quite even. He moved on. The third bowl held shriveled apricots, their skins cracked and dark as old leather, and when he bent close he caught the faintest sweetness still breathing from the wrinkled flesh. He picked one up, turned it in his fingers, then set it back.

The chamber turned at its corner. Four more shrines crowded the short arm, two per wall, and at the far end stood a Worm Locker beside the last shrine: a narrow upright box of blackened wood, its seams packed with calcite, white and hard as dried spit. An iron slit ran across its face at eye level.

Something inside was making a sound.

Igon pressed his visor to the iron slit. Inside, a skeleton hung by pins driven through its wrists and ankles. The rust had fused to the bone, orange bleeding into white like a stain soaked through linen. The jaw was working. No tendons held it. It dropped and caught, dropped and caught, clicking at each downstroke, and from between the yellow teeth came a single sustained note.

He knew the melody. His hand tightened on the hilt, then loosened. He looked at his own gauntlet as if it belonged to someone else.

"Well," said Igon. His breath fogged the inside of the visor and beaded on the iron. "You have something to say."

He leaned close and hummed. Low, warm, matching the pitch. The note inside the locker swelled. The skeleton's jaw widened. The ribcage rattled against its pins, dry cartilage popping like knuckles cracked underwater, and the note split into a voice.

Thin. Reedy. Pitched like a finger drawn across wet glass.

"What does it feel like," said the voice. No inflection. Not a question.

The left pin tore free of the wrist. Bone and rust came with it. The skeleton lunged against the remaining pins and the locker's wood groaned, and Igon stepped back and raised the claymore in both hands.

"Right," he said. "None of that."

The second wrist pin snapped. The skeleton folded forward through the slit, its torso threading the gap like a snake leaving its skin, ribs scraping iron. Igon brought the claymore down on the exposed spine. Glass struck calcium. The sound was wet and clean, like a bell rung in milk, and the vertebrae separated. The upper half dropped; a scatter of finger bones skittered across the stone. The lower half hung from its ankle pins, swaying.

The upper half dragged itself forward on ruined hands. Igon planted his boot on the skull and pushed until something gave beneath the sole. The jaw clicked twice more. Stopped.

The note faded from the chamber walls.

Igon lifted his boot. The skull was flattened on one side, the jaw wrenched loose and lying apart from the face like a dropped tool. He wiped the sole against the stone floor.

"Not the song I came for," he said.

Two crystal doors flanked the far wall. His lantern's glow touched them both and pale green light scattered across the shrine faces like sunlight through pond water. He crossed to the nearer door, pressed his palm flat against the surface, and felt nothing. No vibration. No warmth.

He hummed.

The crystal answered. Green light pooled in the seams and the door slid wide, and somewhere deeper in the tohmb another note began, faint and sustained, waiting for him to match it.

Igon walked through.
