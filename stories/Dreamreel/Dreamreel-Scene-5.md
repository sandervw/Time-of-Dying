# Dreamreel: Scene 5

The door opened onto grey, not darkness — the false-sun globe above the central gallery having long since emptied of whatever luminance it once held, its dead face casting light that was not so much light as the negation of shadow, flat and without warmth or direction, so that Othelmedir's boot found the first flagstone's raised corner before his eyes had adjusted. He stumbled, yet straighted instantly, without comment, without breaking stride, as though he were familiar with such terrain.

Weirmoth moved along the balcony rail, one hand running along the stone — cold even through his glove, the drop to the dead garden below shifting in and out of view through flat pillars as he walked — while Othelmedir examined the shelves. His hand moved from shelf to shelf, and then stopped. The spine gave under his fingers, soft and yielding, its cover having absorbed so many seasons of damp that it had become a texture rather than a structure. He drew it carefully free from its neighbors. The volume was heavier than one would expect from its mold-thinned cover. Othelmedir opened this tome to a random page. The ink had been preserved, at least.

A sour taste - given off by the ankle-high gas - suffused this library. It was something at the edge of rancid, which settled at the back of the throat and lingered.

"That one worth reading?" Weirmoth had stopped at the rail, watching Othelmedir.

"It is a treatise, Weirmoth," Othelmedir rejoined. He closed the book. "Which is to say it repays study rather than light perusal, and this particular treatise — *Potions and Poisons of Eleven Reagents* — would repay more study than most of the volumes which have kept it company."

"The gas," said Weirmoth. "You were going to tell me how it works."

"The cauldron in this tower is stoppered, yet elements seep regardless, as a sealed vessel of brine will still sweat salt through its seams, and those elements dispersing outward are what produce the Dreamreel. The deaths the villagers have been dreaming are not visions but residue." Othelmedir moved to a short spiral stair. Weirmoth followed. They descended to the library's lower floor, and turned to regard a door half-hidden behind a shelf. "The still-room lies behind that door."

Then they heard it a sound. It came from somewhere beyond the door; a low, sustained sound of something drawing breath after long stillness came through the wood.

Othelmedir did not move at once. He stood holding the treatise. Weirmoth stood a little farther back, in the center of the open central space below the dead sun, among the garden plots: small humped islands of pale, grassless soil amidst the ankle-deep sea of gas - and each island marked where the cabal had buried one of its mancers.

Othelmedir tucked the treatise under his arm and regarded Weirmoth with an unreadable expression.

"Weirmoth," he said. "Your metier, I think."
