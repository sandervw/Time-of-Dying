# Dreamreel: Scene 4

Othelmedir turned to the threshold. The gas had crept past the sill and was working down the steps in a slow, knee-high tide. The taste of it reached him first, meat-thick on the back of the tongue. He swallowed.

Weirmoth tucked the canary into his cloak and stood also, rolling his shoulders. He shook the spear out of its wrap. He spat once into the gas and watched the spit pool.

"After you, then," said Weirmoth.

Othelmedir stepped down. Spider silk furred the lintel in a grey patina, and his sleeve drew a dark bruise across it as he passed under.

The first chamber was low-ceilinged and round, and the gas lay in it knee-deep and pale green, and through the gas the bodies stood at their old stations.

There were four of them, by his count, propped against pillars or sagged at the foot of a lectern, and the gas lapped at their thighs and slack hands. They wore the leather and ringed mail of the Milkmoot breach, and most still held their weapons. One had a hornet-husk fragment caked to the side of his jaw, brown and brittle; Ostrobone's sign, or what was left of someone who had stood near him.

The bodies began to move.

It was not quickly done. A hand closed on a haft. A head lifted. The nearest, a broad man with a thin grey beard, took a step forward and his mouth opened and closed and opened, shaping words without sound. Othelmedir watched the lips. He did not speak. He stepped back and put his shoulder against the pillar nearest the door.

Weirmoth came past him on the right with the spear lowered.

The first thrust took the bearded man under the jaw and out the back of the neck, and Weirmoth twisted the shaft and stepped through and the body folded into the gas. Two more were on him by then. He took the second through the soft of the belly, levered, and let the corpse drag itself off the spear by its own weight; the third he put down with the butt-spike to the temple, twice. The skull went in the second time. The spear came back to guard between every kill.

Othelmedir moved past the lectern as Weirmoth dropped a fourth.

Three common books lay open on the lectern, all to a single page: *On the Conduct of Sealed Houses*, *Compendium of Threshold Speech*, and *A Practitioner's Almanac*. The spines were cracked flat. The page had not been turned in a long while. He did not pause. He went around the lectern and through the doorway beyond, and Weirmoth came after him still wiping the spearhead on a fold of his cloak.

"You been told this floorplan," Weirmoth said. "Or you got a nose for it."

"I have a sense for sealed houses," Othelmedir said. "Walk in front of me where the corridor splits, Weirmoth, and to the right at the second turning."

"Right at the second."

Two more of the dead were on them in the next chamber. Weirmoth speared one through the eye and the other through the meat under the arm, where the mail had gapped, and the body went down sighing gas out of its lungs. Othelmedir knelt beside it and drew aside the collar. A pendant lay against the breastbone: hammered tin, the shape of a flat tear, the sigil of a lidded cauldron with one open eye on the lid. He worked the cord over the dead man's head and put the pendant into his cloak.

The corpse's mouth was still working. Othelmedir did not look at the mouth.

He noted instead, in the cold ledger he kept for such things, that the gas had served the deeper tissues better than the shallow. Skin had gone to a uniform old wax; eyes had collapsed. But the muscles under the skin held. Tendons held. The throat-meat that drove the silent jaws held best of all. Useful to know. He stood.

A seventh was waiting at the column.

This one Weirmoth took with a short clean thrust through the hollow of the throat. The body slid down the column and came to rest sitting, and the gas rose to its chin. A ledger was nailed to the column above the body, a strip of vellum a forearm long, names down the left and duty-hours down the right; the most recent hour set against the most recent name was older than either man in the room. Othelmedir read down the list once. He went on.

The corridor narrowed and bent left, and the gas thinned to ankle-depth, and at the bend it stopped being a corridor at all.

Othelmedir put his hand out and rested his palm against the surface which faced them.

The wall was warm. It was a weave of wet pink fibers laid crosswise and lengthwise over the passage from floor to vault, thick as a man's arm where the strands crossed, throbbing under his hand at a slower rhythm than a heart. Between the fibers the gas pressed faintly through and gave up its meat-smell on the near side. He pushed. The wall pushed back, sluggish, alive.

Weirmoth came up beside him with the spear half-raised and looked, and then let the spear dip low again.

"It will not be opened from this side, Weirmoth, by any spear of yours, nor by any blade I have ever known to be carried into such a house."

And the wall continued to contract and swell under Othelmedir's palm.
