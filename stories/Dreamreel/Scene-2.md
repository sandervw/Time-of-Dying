# Contumelious-Ectogenesis: Scene 2 — Teammates

## Full Scene

The common room of *The Hooded Pillow* had been emptied by sleep before sundown, and Othelmedir, who had asked for the highest room and been given it, came down again on the pretext of bread, ducking through the inner doorway whose lintel had been rehung an inch lower than the standing height of any reasonable man. He set his shoulder under it as one sets a shoulder under a yoke, and emerged into a room that smelled of tallow gone soft, of beer-mash souring under the boards, of an onion broth that had been steeping somewhere out of sight since noon and still had not been carried out. At the far bench the bedmeister, Noseman Under Eaves, was already in conference with another guest, leaned across the trestle in the posture of a man who has spent the last of his patience and is now spending the patience of his employer; and the guest, sitting opposite, had the look of a roadman who had washed his hands twice for every league he had walked, and who intended to do so again before the evening closed. Othelmedir, whose business with the bedmeister was quieter and could wait, took the next bench down and listened.

"Help me out, master adventurer," Noseman said, low. "Hear them through the walls?"

"Heard them," said the other man, and the other man, in saying it, palmed something pale and waxy from his sleeve and rolled it through the wash of his thumb and forefinger as one rolls a bead. "Hear nothing else, mind, when my hand is clean. My mother's lesson, by her elbow, by her ear."

Noseman's own thumb was elsewhere, on a bead-tally cord knotted at his hip; he worked it without looking, one bead, one bead, one bead, as if counting out the village's nightly ration of trouble. "It is the dreams," he said. "We call it Dreamreel."

"Reel," said the man. "Like a thread."

"Like a thread. Like a worry." Noseman set both palms on the boards. "Lie down. Try sleep. A small worry, like grit. Think of better things. Grit again, in the head. Think of warm bread. Grit at the eyelid. Think of your wife's neck. Grit, then a face. An unknown face. His death, in your skull. Again. Again. A recital." He drew breath. "Help, master."

The man across the trestle leaned back, and the bench, which had one short leg, tipped under him until the spear leaned beside it knocked the wall and rang under its oilcloth wrap. He laughed, and the laugh was confident; he caught the spear at the throat and set it to balance again. "Weirmoth, by name. The spear, you ask of? Saint's tool, once. Lanced a poisoned bishop's neck, drained the abscess, hummed thereafter when sickness was near. Hums faint just now, by my ear. Nothing my ear has not heard before."

His cloak fell open as he reached, and the inner seam ran, plainly and wrongly, against the outer; a corner of it bore the embroidered initial of someone whose funeral shroud had once been larger. His knuckles were grey; his nails were clean as a notary's.

A draft came down the chimney, cold, smelling of old soot, and the chimney did not take it. Othelmedir, watching the half-grown mutton-chop on Weirmoth's lip catch the draft and shiver, rose, and named himself.

"Master bedmeister Noseman, master adventurer Weirmoth," he said, with the courtesy due each in turn, "if I have understood the bedmeister's account of the affliction, which he names the Dreamreel and which he likens to a worry that consumes the sleep around it, then the difficulty he describes is one for which I claim a particular and unfashionable competence; I am, for my faults, a specialist in dream-mancy, and I would offer my assistance to your enterprise, master adventurer, if your enterprise will have me."

Weirmoth looked up at him, and up. "Tall, you. Use of you, certainly. Welcome to the work, by the elbow, by the road." He turned to Noseman. "Coin?"

The bedmeister did not stack it. He pushed across the boards a square-cut copper, then another, then another, sliding each one along the grain until they made a row of three at Weirmoth's wrist; he pushed no more. "Morning. Both, at the door. A pair."

"Morning," Othelmedir said, restating the bedmeister's word as he restated most words, and laid his gloved hands on the table's edge, gloves the colour of wet bark. "Master Weirmoth, on the matter of my fee, you should know that I work by barter and oath, not coin." Noseman's thumb moved on the cord, and did not stop.
