# Dreamreel: Scene 6

Weirmoth went in low with his thrust. The spear took the dead-dreamer under the floating rib, a clean upward stab meant for the heart; but the thing did not buckle, only twisted on the haft and clouted him across the temple with the flat of its forearm. Weirmoth staggered, and the corpse stepped through the swing, and its other hand closed about his throat. He went back against the slate worktop. Wax-sealed jars chimed at his hip. The spear was pinned between their bodies, useless. Then Weirmoth let the haft drop, slipped one shoulder, dragged a poniard from his sleeve, and drove it three times into the soft place above the collarbone. The grip on his throat went slack. He recovered the spear, then planted a hard, final thrust through the hollow of the jaw.

Othelmedir stepped forward. He laid the treatise open on a clean square of slate and weighted its pages with two iron weights shaped like sleeping hounds. He took down the copper alembic from its tripod and bled a vague liquid off through a side-cock until the bowl was empty. Then he began.

Widowwine first, two thumbs deep, the colour of bruised plums. Priest's quicklime ground fresh on a side-stone; a crumb of grit caught beneath his thumbnail and stayed there as he worked. Honey from a sealed crock, pale as a milkmaid's freshly-laundered linens. He fed the iron shavings into the honey in a slow trickle, and they hissed and turned the viscous gold to graphite at the contact. Green bled from a fistful of grass-blades crushed against the slate. Black lye. Glasswort. Dandelion root, scraped to a pulp with the edge of his thumb. He touched the crystalwort to the tip of his tongue before adding it; briny, faintly metallic, the correct salt. Last went a tiny, perfect cube of nine-day-gnats suspended in amber.

Across the chamber by the wall Weirmoth had seated himself on a warped oak bench. He threaded a bone needle through the long tear in his shroud-cloth. His fingers were immaculately clean, he had scrubbed them with his soap immediately post-battle. He drew the stitches small and even, watching Othelmedir over the rise and fall of his hand.

Othelmedir went among the shelves. He took down a jar marked in a fading script, weighed it, replaced it. He took down another, broke its wax, and tipped a measure of cinerous crystals into the inner pocket of his cloak. He did this with three further jars. He returned to the cauldron and stirred.

"That last one," Weirmoth said. "Those crystals. What's that which sits in your pocket now, and what'll it buy me?"

"The grey," said Othelmedir, "buy nothing of your interest, Weirmoth. It is purchasing for me a future hour in a city not this one. The brew before us is the article of our contract; what travels in my coat is severance against the road home, and your coin is no thinner for it."

Weirmoth bit the thread off against his teeth. "Mancer's pockets get awful deep when no one's counting."

"Mancer's pockets get awful deep when no one is counting; just so. Count, then." Othelmedir did not turn. "When the cauldron is split, you may search me to the lining. For now, attend. There is an eleventh reagent the treatise names: *Respiration from a Silver Dream* — a breath drawn from a sleeper who has died inside the sleep. The gas in this hall preserved such a breath; the first death it took, that of an anonymous trespasser, hangs in the air. To fetch it I must enter the dream that holds it, and I cannot. A practitioner habituated to necromancy who drinks the witness-draught burns at the nerves and is rendered into ash. An unhabituated man passes through and wakes."

Weirmoth set the needle down. He looked at the cup Othelmedir was now decanting, then at Othelmedir's white hand on the ladle, then back at the cup.

"Drink, master Weirmoth," said Othelmedir, holding out the cup now. "You will return, with the taste of wine still upon your lips; and we will go down to the cauldron together, with the potion rendered from this reagent."

Weirmoth took the cup. He held it at the level of his chin.

"I am not so clever as you take me for, friend," he said.

Then he raised the cup to his lips.
