# Dreamreel: Scene 3

## Full Scene

The bluff path was loose shale that turned beneath the boot, and Weirmoth, climbing it ahead of him with the deliberate looseness of a man who wished to be thought casual, slipped twice in the first hundred paces, righted himself each time without comment, and then, on the third slip, swore softly into the cloth at his shoulder as a man swears into his own collar when he has tired of being heard. Othelmedir followed at the older man's pace. He carried nothing but the chalk on his hands and the long grey stole of his order folded once across the forearm, and he watched, without seeming to, the inner edge of Weirmoth's left bootheel, which was worn down at an angle that no honest road had ever made.

The Tower of the Attercop sat on the bald crown of the bluff like a tooth set crooked in a jaw. It was lower than memory had kept it. The sweating stone had taken on, in the years since his hand had last touched it, the colour of a wet hide, and along the north footstone a rim of frost had survived the morning, narrow as a fingernail, white against the grey.

Weirmoth stopped a pace from the door and laid a hand on his lower back as if to find something there.

"My head is full of someone else's grandmother this morning," he said. "I slept poor as a beggar's bench, and the bench did most of the talking."

"Someone else's grandmother, Master Weirmoth," Othelmedir said, turning the words back to him as one turns a stone to see the underside, "though only last night, by my hearing, you boasted that dreams had given you up entirely as a bad client."

Weirmoth showed his teeth in what he intended for a grin. "My ribs ached. The bedstraw at the Pillow has lice the size of a man's thumbnail. I dreamt nothing worth the keeping."

"Nothing worth the keeping," Othelmedir said. "That is a careful phrasing for a man who keeps so little."

He stepped past the sellsword and laid his ungloved palm flat on the door. The cold of the stone came up through the cloth at the small of his back at once, a slow draught with a season in it, and the air close to the seam smelled, faintly and unmistakably, of wet copper and of hay long past the summer that had cut it. The seal itself was a crust of grey wax pressed into one rune, paler than the stone around it, and someone unfamiliar with the work would have taken it for a cooper's mark.

"Stand off a pace," he said, without looking back. "I set this seam, Master Weirmoth, in another year and under another name, and I would have it come away clean."

Weirmoth stepped back. He did not ask which year. He watched the older man's mouth.

Othelmedir bent his head close to the wax, whispered, _"Öltesoldzo sjhaj,"_ and felt the brief, ember-warmth of the deadspeech leave his lips and pass into the rune. _With a sigh came loose the sutures._ The wax did not crack so much as soften and lift, in the way a scar lifts under steam, and from the seam below it there began to pour, low and slow, a greenish vapour that moved like cream poured the wrong way up the slope, and the cobwebs in the inner frame, which no draught had touched, began to quiver on their lines. A fat house-spider lowered itself a thumb's-length, considered the world, and climbed back.

"By the Pew's own teeth," Weirmoth breathed, and then, hearing himself, closed his mouth on the rest.

"By the Red Pew, you mean to say," Othelmedir said, and did not turn his head. "An old oath for a sellsword. Step further back, Master Weirmoth. The first fume is the worst of it."

They stepped back together to wait for the first fume to thin. From the village below the only sound that carried up was a loose shutter knocking at irregular intervals, a small domestic complaint laid under an unholy thing.

"By the historic knowledge that is my trade, Master Weirmoth," Othelmedir said, watching the green run, "if this gas keeps pouring through another season the villagers will not be remembering their own grandmothers at all. Other people's deaths will have moved in entirely, room by room."

Weirmoth nodded as if at a price quoted, and then, listening to the shutter, drew from inside his shroud-cloak a small thing wrapped in old altar-felt and unwrapped it on his palm. The canary's throat had been sewn shut in tiny black stitches. Its wings were folded as if it had only just lit upon a perch.

"Mister Ribbon never did take to Milkmoot weather in the throat," Weirmoth said into the bird's stitched neck, soft as a man at a confessional, and then, hearing himself a second time, looked up and tried for the grin again. It did not quite come.
