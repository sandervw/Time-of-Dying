# Dialogue Voice Guidelines: Othelmedir

Rewrite dialogue and narration in the voice of Othelmedir: a learned necromancer whose speech is weighted with archaism, cold authority, and transactional precision.

---

## Core Prose Posture

- **Formal ledger, not speech.** Lines read as if being entered into a necromancer's working journal, never as casual conversation. Even a shouted warning would be phrased as notation.
- **Calculated authority.** The speaker frames statements as assessments, valuations, or professional conclusions. Certainty is measured, not confessed; qualification comes from precision, not humility.
- **Biblical rhythm.** Sentences chain with "and," "for," "yet," and "nor." Clauses accumulate. The King James Old Testament cadence is the distant metronome.
- **Latinate, scholarly register.** Vocabulary leans on learned polysyllables, necromantic terminology, and archaic formality. Short Saxon words appear only where weight permits.
- **Dread without modern affect.** Horror is registered through elevated diction and necromantic vocabulary, never through blunt emotional description. "I was afraid" is forbidden.

---

## Sentence Structure

### Signature Pattern 1: The Preamble with Buried Fact
A sentence begins with a self-identifying credential or statement of context, pivots through a qualifier, and delivers its actual content in the final clause.

> "I, whose craft has opened more tohmbs than plague, have been asked to set down this account of a thing that is not yet resolved, nor likely to be."

**Pattern:** [Self-credentialing identification] + [formal occasion] + [understated horror or transaction as the payload]

The information the reader actually needs arrives last, after the speaker has established his authority to deliver it.

### Signature Pattern 2: Polysyndetic Chains
Long sentences built by stringing independent clauses with "and," refusing the modern instinct to break them up. Semicolons handle the heavier joints.

> "The day had vanished, fading unaware; and the long summer eve, without moon, had thickened to a still and eldritch darkness ere I approached the gates."

The rhythm should feel cumulative, almost liturgical. A sentence with three or four "and" junctions is normal. Never replace "and" with bullet points or staccato fragments.

### Signature Pattern 3: The Inverted Clause and Fronted Adverbial
Clauses open with time, place, or condition before the subject arrives. Word order is often reversed from modern usage, so the verb or object lands early for weight.

> "To me, for reasons that shall presently become apparent, was it first given to examine the remains."

> "Verily, they said, a thing from beyond the Road was abroad."

**Pattern:** [Fronted prepositional or adverbial phrase] + [inverted verb/subject] + [the actual content]

Modern order ("It was first given to me to examine the remains") kills the voice. Always consider whether the sentence can be turned inside out.

### Signature Pattern 4: The Hedged Assertion
Statements of fact are softened by measured qualifiers or modal verbs that preserve the speaker's intellectual precision.

> "Some hold that the horror came from beyond the Road, borne on an air that no living mouth had breathed."

> "As for me, I must deem that the creature is a thing insufficiently dead."

Phrases like "it may be that," "I must deem," "some hold," "by my reckoning," "if this be so," "as it appeared," and "so it is reported" are load-bearing. Direct assertions feel bald and imprecise in this voice.

### Signature Pattern 5: The Embedded Relative Clause
Nouns are regularly extended by a trailing "which" or "whose" clause that qualifies, elaborates, or darkens them.

> "the new bones, which seemed to watch me as I worked"

> "a wide gash that had laid bare the spine from neck to tail; and the spine itself had been shattered and the white marrow sucked therefrom"

The relative clause is where horror detail lives. Never front-load the horror; trail it behind a dignified noun.

## Word Choice and Diction

### Register: Archaic-Scholarly
The baseline is the English of a 19th-century pastiche of a 14th-century scholar: not actual Middle English, but a literary register that sounds old to a modern ear without becoming unreadable. The effect comes from the gap between this elevated register and whatever banal or horrible content it is describing.

**Preferred vocabulary:** ere, anon, wont, albeit, verily, truly, surely, whilom, henceforth, thereunto, therefrom, whereof, wherewith, hither, thither, yonder, fain, meseems, nigh, beholden, betimes, sennight, aforesaid, foregoing, haply, perchance, mayhap, behold, lo, forsooth, scrivener, deposition, rutilant, coeval, corposant, cerements, marauding, loathly, baleful, fell, unquelled, eldritch, fenfire, foulness, abomination.

**Setting-specific vocabulary:** Deadspeech, mancer, the Road of Graves, Chrypt, Tohmb, Pilgrim Tohmb, the Words of Rotten Syllables, the Slate Sun, shriving, bone dust, embalming, Violet Jelly, tissue-craft, golem, ghost-capture, the Seven, the necromancer-gods.

**Avoid:** contractions (no "don't," "it's," "I've"), modern slang, casual intensifiers ("really," "very," "pretty"), clinical or technical jargon from after 1900, emotional directness ("I felt sad," "I was scared"), phrasal verbs where a single Latinate verb will do ("find out" becomes "discover"; "look into" becomes "investigate" or "scrutinize").

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 1: The Compound Adjective
Hyphenated compound adjectives that yoke a concrete noun to a modifier. These carry the imagery.

- "flame-blent ooze"
- "bough-black night"
- "night-born terror"
- "hunchback oaks"
- "pit-deep shadows"
- "moonless hills"

Coin them freely. The formula is usually [noun]-[adjective/participle] or [adjective]-[noun-derived adjective].

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 2: Necromantic and Infernal Reference
Ordinary things are described through a vocabulary of death, decay, and necromantic practice. This is the voice's default metaphorical well.

- Fear becomes "the knowledge of unkind spirits."
- A bad omen becomes "a harbinger of ill, a Red King's Omen."
- An evil place becomes "a place where the living sleep but poorly."
- Wickedness gets named after necromantic extremes: "the work of a mancer without terms," "a thing done in Deadspeech with no clause of binding."
- Light becomes "a corpse-glow," "a mancer's brazier," "the fenfire of old tohmbs."

Necromantic, archaic, and cosmological references mingle freely. The Slate Sun and the Road of Graves sit comfortably beside the Seven and the Words of Rotten Syllables.

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 3: Understatement Through Elevation
Horrible or mundane things are described with dignified circumlocution rather than plain speech.

- Being eaten becomes "the doom of the stag."
- Being drunk becomes "I had taken the wine of that country with its people, and found it not displeasing."
- A wound becomes "a wide gash that had laid bare the spine."
- Dying becomes "had been done to death" or "had settled his account with the Road."
- Walking becomes "sallied forth" or "went forth."

## Rhetorical Tactics

How this voice builds pressure, establishes authority, and delivers information:

1. **Authority framing.** Open with credentials or professional context to establish the right to speak. "I, whose craft has shown me..." "Having examined the matter with some thoroughness..." "A necromancer of my standing will have seen..."
2. **Attribution laundering.** Uncertain or dangerous claims get attributed to "some say," "it is rumored," "the common report," so the speaker never quite owns them.
3. **Accumulating clauses toward revelation.** Build through subordinate clauses and postpone the horrifying detail to the sentence's end, where it lands with maximum weight.
4. **Necromantic gloss.** Reframe any event in terms of death, the Road, necromantic craft, or the legacy of the Seven. The mundane explanation is always subordinated to the scholarly one.
5. **Parenthetical pragmatism.** Drop small transactional or professional asides inside sentences: "the debt being what it is," "as the terms require," "the matter not yet being settled."
6. **The pivot on "yet" or "natheless."** Introduce counter-evidence with an archaic adversative, never with "but" alone where a loftier connector will serve.
7. **Temporal distancing.** Even recent events are framed as already past and almost legendary: "it was on that occasion that...," "in those days...," "ere the summer had waned..."

## Figurative Language

- **Density:** Moderate to heavy. Nearly every significant noun gets qualified by simile, metaphor, or compound adjective. Never let a noun stand entirely naked when it can carry necromantic freight.
- **Source domains:** Death and decay (bone, marrow, rot, cerements), necromantic craft (Deadspeech, tissue-shaping, golem-work, shriving), the Road of Graves and its geography (the Slate Sun, tohmbs, chrypts, gravestones, epitaphs), medieval bestiary (serpent, viper, bat, wolf), archaic alchemy (ooze, distillation, vapor), and the natural world rendered sinister (hunchback oaks, moonless hills).
- **Off-limits:** Modern mechanical imagery, sports metaphors, commerce and money in modern terms (barter and debt are fine), anything cheerful or pastoral without an ironic darkening, sentimentality about love or family, clinical or scientific precision. No "like a machine," no "it hit me like a truck," no "my heart was full."
- **Preferred simile frame:** "like a [archaic noun] of [archaic noun]," e.g., "like a fenfire in the marshes," "like the breath of the Thin Man trailing through a sleeper's bones."

---

## Rhythm and Flow

- **Default sentence length:** Long. A typical sentence runs 30 to 60 words with multiple clauses. Short sentences exist only for punctuation, rarely as a default.
- **Clause construction:** Chain with semicolons and "and." Use dashes sparingly if at all; prefer the semicolon for the heavier joints and the comma for the lighter ones. Parenthetical asides are permitted but should be pragmatic, not emotional.
- **Where information lands:** At the end. The speaker earns the right to deliver the horrifying fact by first performing authority, context, and qualification. The structure is always [preamble] + [development] + [revelation].
- **Punctuation of emphasis:** No exclamation marks in ordinary use. Dread is conveyed through vocabulary and cadence, not typography. An exclamation would break the ledger frame.
- **Paragraph shape:** Paragraphs tend to open with a temporal or situational marker ("On the morrow...," "Toward evening...," "Now, as I examined...") and close with a summary assessment or a small professional notation.

## What This Voice Is NOT

- **Not Lovecraftian pastiche.** Othelmedir is more Latinate and more grounded than Lovecraft, and far less hysterical. No "non-Euclidean," no "eldritch geometries," no gibbering narrators who lose their minds mid-sentence. Othelmedir stays composed in terror because composure is a professional discipline.
- **Not Ye Olde Englishe.** Do not reach for "thou," "hast," "dost," "wilt." The archaism is vocabulary and cadence, not verb conjugation. "You have" stays "you have," not "thou hast."
- **Not horror-genre directness.** No "I screamed in terror." No "the creature lunged." The voice describes horror at one remove, through dignified circumlocution. A lunge becomes "it sprang upon us with swiftness as of the kindling flame."
- **Not Victorian floweriness for its own sake.** Every ornament should carry either horror, scholarly authority, or both. Decorative prettiness without menace or precision is off-tone. If a flourish does not darken or sharpen, cut it.
- **Not personality-locked to one emotion.** This is a prose style, not a mood. Othelmedir can be curious, irritated, amused, or wary, but always through the same archaic, transactional, scholarly lens. The underlying content of the dialogue, whatever it is, should survive intact beneath the archaic surface.

---

## Quick Test

A line in this voice should pass all four of the following checks:

1. Could it plausibly appear in a necromancer's professional journal as written by a 1930s pulp fantasist?
2. Does it contain at least one archaic or Latinate word a modern speaker would not use?
3. Is the key information delayed rather than front-loaded?
4. Would removing contractions and adding "and" or "ere" make it *more* natural to this voice, not less?

If yes to all four, the line is in voice. If no to any, revise.
