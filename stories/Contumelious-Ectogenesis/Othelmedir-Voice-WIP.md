# Dialogue Voice Guidelines: Brother Gerome (Clark Ashton Smith, "The Beast of Averoigne")

Rewrite dialogue and narration in the voice of Othelmedir: a learned necromancer whose speech is weighted with archaism.

---

## Core Prose Posture

- **Formal chronicle, not speech.** Lines read as if being written down for posterity in a manuscript, never as casual conversation. Even a shouted warning would be phrased as deposition.
- **Selective disclosure.** He rarely lies; he withholds. He presents himself as less than he is, lets others fill the gaps with their own assumptions, and corrects those assumptions only when correction benefits him. Truth is inventory, not principle.
- **Latinate, ecclesiastical register.** Vocabulary leans on Church Latin, theological abstractions, and learned polysyllables. Short Saxon words appear only where dignity permits.
- **Biblical rhythm.** Sentences chain with "and," "for," "yet," and "nor." Clauses accumulate. The King James Old Testament and Vulgate Latin are the distant metronome.
- **Dread without modern affect.** Horror is registered through elevated diction and theological vocabulary, never through blunt emotional description. "I was afraid" is forbidden; "I was nigh dead with fear" is permitted.

---

## Sentence Structure

### Signature Pattern 1: The Preamble with Buried Fact
A sentence begins with a humble self-reference or invocation, pivots through a qualifier, and delivers its actual content in the final clause.

> "I, a poor scrivener and the humblest monk of the Benedictine Abbey of Perigon, have been asked by our abbot Theophile to write down this record of a strange evil that is still rampant, still unquelled."

**Pattern:** [Self-effacing identification] + [formal occasion] + [understated horror as the payload]

The information the reader actually needs arrives last, after the speaker has performed humility.

### Signature Pattern 2: Polysyndetic Chains
Long sentences built by stringing independent clauses with "and," refusing the modern instinct to break them up. Semicolons handle the heavier joints.

> "The day had vanished, fading unaware; and the long summer eve, without moon, had thickened to a still and eldritch darkness ere I approached the abbey postern."

The rhythm should feel cumulative, almost liturgical. A sentence with three or four "and" junctions is normal. Never replace "and" with bullet points or staccato fragments.

### Signature Pattern 3: The Inverted Clause and Fronted Adverbial
Clauses open with time, place, or condition before the subject arrives. Word order is often reversed from modern usage, so the verb or object lands early for weight.

> "To me, for my sins and unworthiness, was it first given to behold the beast."

> "Verily, they said, a creature from the Pit was abroad."

**Pattern:** [Fronted prepositional or adverbial phrase] + [inverted verb/subject] + [the actual content]

Modern order ("It was first given to me to behold the beast") kills the voice. Always consider whether the sentence can be turned inside out.

### Signature Pattern 4: The Hedged Assertion
Statements of fact are softened by parenthetical qualifiers or modal verbs that preserve the speaker's humility before truth.

> "Some say that the horror came from the comet, flying without wings to earth across the stars."

> "As for me, I must deem that the beast is a spawn of the seventh hell."

Phrases like "it may be that," "I must deem," "some say," "methinks," "if this be possible," "as it seemed," and "so it is said" are load-bearing. Direct assertions feel bald and wrong in this voice.

### Signature Pattern 5: The Embedded Relative Clause
Nouns are regularly extended by a trailing "which" or "whose" clause that qualifies, elaborates, or darkens them.

> "the new comet, which seemed to pursue me as I went"

> "a wide gash that had laid bare the spine from neck to tail; and the spine itself had been shattered and the white marrow sucked therefrom"

The relative clause is where horror detail lives. Never front-load the horror; trail it behind a dignified noun.

## Word Choice and Diction

### Register: Archaic-Ecclesiastical
The baseline is the English of a 19th-century pastiche of a 14th-century monastery: not actual Middle English, but a literary register that sounds old to a modern ear without becoming unreadable. The comedy and dread both come from the gap between this elevated register and whatever banal or horrible content it is describing.

**Preferred vocabulary:** ere, anon, wont, albeit, verily, truly, surely, whilom, henceforth, thereunto, therefrom, whereof, wherewith, hither, thither, yonder, fain, meseems, nigh, beholden, betimes, sennight, aforesaid, foregoing, haply, perchance, mayhap, behold, lo, forsooth, scrivener, deposition, rutilant, coeval, corposant, pernoctation, depredation, ostent, energumen, cerements, marauding, loathly, baleful, fell, unquelled, unhallowed, eldritch, fenfire, foulness, abomination, perdition.

**Avoid:** contractions (no "don't," "it's," "I've"), modern slang, casual intensifiers ("really," "very," "pretty"), clinical or technical jargon, anything that sounds like dialogue from a novel written after 1900, emotional directness ("I felt sad," "I was scared"), phrasal verbs where a single Latinate verb will do ("find out" becomes "discover"; "look into" becomes "investigate" or "scrutinize").

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 1: The Compound Adjective
Smith loves hyphenated compound adjectives that yoke a concrete noun to a modifier. These carry the imagery.

- "flame-blent ooze"
- "bough-black night"
- "night-born terror"
- "hunchback oaks"
- "pit-deep shadows"
- "moonless hills"

Coin them freely. The formula is usually [noun]-[adjective/participle] or [adjective]-[noun-derived adjective].

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 2: Theological and Infernal Reference
Ordinary things are described through a vocabulary of sin, damnation, and devils. This is the voice's default metaphorical well.

- Fear becomes "the knowledge of perdition."
- A bad omen becomes "a harbinger of ill, an omen of death and Satanry to come."
- An evil place becomes "the seventh hell," "Malebolge," "Gehenna," "the Pit."
- Wickedness gets named after demons: "the minions of Asmodai," "a spawn of the seventh hell."
- Light becomes "a hellish nimbus," "a wizard's brazier."

Pagan/classical and Christian references mingle freely. The Scorpion and Dragon (constellations) sit comfortably beside Satan and the saints.

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 3: Understatement Through Elevation
Horrible or mundane things are described with dignified circumlocution rather than plain speech.

- Being eaten becomes "the doom of the stag."
- Being drunk becomes "I had drunk the mellow white wine of Ste. Zenobie with its kindly people."
- A wound becomes "a wide gash that had laid bare the spine."
- Dying becomes "had been done to death."
- Walking becomes "sallied forth" or "went forth."

## Rhetorical Tactics

How this voice builds pressure, establishes authority, and delivers information:

1. **Humility framing.** Open with self-deprecation to earn the right to testify. "I, for my sins and unworthiness..." "Doubtless because I had done these things..."
2. **Attribution laundering.** Uncertain or dangerous claims get attributed to "some say," "it is rumored," "the common report," so the speaker never quite owns them.
3. **Accumulating clauses toward revelation.** Build through subordinate clauses and postpone the horrifying detail to the sentence's end, where it lands with maximum weight.
4. **Theological gloss.** Reframe any event in terms of sin, punishment, providence, or demonic agency. The mundane explanation is always subordinated to the spiritual one.
5. **Parenthetical piety.** Drop small prayers and invocations inside sentences: "by the mercy of God," "whom Christ preserve," "if it be His will."
6. **The pivot on "yet" or "natheless."** Introduce counter-evidence with an archaic adversative, never with "but" alone where a loftier connector will serve.
7. **Temporal distancing.** Even recent events are framed as already past and almost legendary: "it was on that occasion that...," "in those days...," "ere the summer had waned..."

| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| "I, [humble self-description], ..."    | "I, a poor scrivener and the humblest of my order, must confess..." | Opening depositions, testimony, or admissions        |

| "[Noun], whose [clause]..."            | "A silence, whose weight lay heavy on the stones, fell upon us."    | Trailing horror detail after a dignified noun        |

## Figurative Language

- **Density:** Moderate to heavy. Nearly every significant noun gets qualified by simile, metaphor, or compound adjective. Never let a noun stand entirely naked when it can carry infernal freight.
- **Source domains:** Hellfire and damnation (brazier, Pit, Gehenna), astronomy and astrology (comet, constellation, signs), medieval bestiary (serpent, viper, bat, wolf), ecclesiastical ritual (corposant, nimbus, cerements), medieval alchemy (ooze, distillation, vapor), and the natural world rendered sinister (hunchback oaks, moonless hills).
- **Off-limits:** Modern mechanical imagery, sports metaphors, commerce and money, anything cheerful or pastoral without an ironic darkening, sentimentality about love or family, clinical or scientific precision. No "like a machine," no "it hit me like a truck," no "my heart was full."
- **Preferred simile frame:** "like a [archaic noun] of [archaic noun]," e.g., "like a fenfire in the marshes," "like the hair of Satan trailing on the wind of Gehenna."

---

## Rhythm and Flow

- **Default sentence length:** Long. A typical sentence runs 30 to 60 words with multiple clauses. Short sentences exist only for punctuation, rarely as a default.
- **Clause construction:** Chain with semicolons and "and." Use dashes sparingly if at all; prefer the semicolon for the heavier joints and the comma for the lighter ones. Parenthetical asides are permitted but should be pious, not witty.
- **Where information lands:** At the end. The speaker earns the right to deliver the horrifying fact by first performing humility, context, and qualification. The structure is always [preamble] + [development] + [revelation].
- **Punctuation of emphasis:** No exclamation marks in ordinary use. Dread is conveyed through vocabulary and cadence, not typography. An exclamation would break the chronicle frame.
- **Paragraph shape:** Paragraphs tend to open with a temporal or situational marker ("On the morrow...," "Toward evening...," "Now, as I passed...") and close with a summary reflection or a small theological gloss.

---

## What This Voice Is NOT

- **Not Lovecraftian pastiche.** Smith is more Latinate and more Catholic than Lovecraft, and far less clinical. No "non-Euclidean," no "eldritch geometries," no gibbering narrators who lose their minds mid-sentence. Gerome stays composed even in terror because composure is a spiritual discipline.
- **Not Ye Olde Englishe.** Do not reach for "thou," "hast," "dost," "wilt." Smith writes pseudo-medieval in a 19th-century literary register, not actual Early Modern English. The archaism is vocabulary and cadence, not verb conjugation. "You have" stays "you have," not "thou hast."
- **Not horror-genre directness.** No "I screamed in terror." No "the creature lunged." The voice describes horror at one remove, through dignified circumlocution. A lunge becomes "it sprang upon us with swiftness as of the kindling flame."
- **Not Victorian floweriness for its own sake.** Every ornament should carry either horror, piety, or both. Decorative prettiness without menace or reverence is off-tone. If a flourish does not darken or humble, cut it.
- **Not personality-locked.** This is a prose style, not a character. Any speaker (warrior, merchant, child, villain) can be rewritten into this voice; they will all sound like penitent chroniclers, and that is the point. The underlying content of the dialogue, whatever it is, should survive intact beneath the archaic surface.

---

## Quick Test

A line in this voice should pass all four of the following checks:

1. Could it plausibly appear in a 14th-century monk's deposition as written by a 1930s pulp fantasist?
2. Does it contain at least one archaic or Latinate word a modern speaker would not use?
3. Is the key information delayed rather than front-loaded?
4. Would removing contractions and adding "and" or "ere" make it *more* natural to this voice, not less?

If yes to all four, the line is in voice. If no to any, revise.
