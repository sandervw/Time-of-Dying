# Dreamreel

## 1. ESSAY

I have come back to Sulky Wend after an interval of years which I will not number here, having lived once in a holding nearby, under a name I no longer answer to, and having seldom set foot within the village proper even then. The hamlets that string themselves like vertebrae along the Road of Graves are, I think, the most static dwellings in the whole pale catalogue of human settlement; their eaves and slumped shrines age slower than the men who sleep beneath them, and a man who flees such a place may find, on returning, that nothing has consented to move except himself.

Many are they that would stamp the man who abdicates such a stable dwelling, whether or not they be acquainted with the circumstances of his departure. Some would that cowardice; others, prudence; the village hagiographers, were any still kept here, would haply call it craft. I myself lean toward the third reading, though I concede the first has its constituency. The pertinent question is perhaps not why a mancer leaves but what he carries with him upon his return.

The Hooded Pillow stands where it stood, sign-board faintly verdigrised, and to its threshold I direct my first steps; the rest will follow in its accustomed order, as such things tend to do.

## 3. LEMMA — The Little Mass of Failing Lamps

![IMAGE: a row of thirteen black tapers in a shuttered chamber, twelve guttered to smoke and one still burning low](images/the-little-mass-of-failing-lamps.png)

A closed-house funeral rite spoken only by the bereaved, drafted within the early mancer guilds for the burial of inner members. It's form is fixed at thirteen verses, each closing with the snuffing of a single taper, until the room stands in full dark. Use is restricted by guild: the verses are never copied into common books, only learned mouth-to-ear, and the wording differs from group-to-group. Outsiders attending are sent from the house before the first lamp is snuffed.

## 4. ESSAY

The Hooded Pillow was, in an earlier reckoning, a lodging accomodation for the staff of that nearby tower; the ledger bound in attercop silk on its counter is itself a cast-off gift from the mancers who resided upon that bluff, presented in some forgotten season as a courtesy or an inducement, depending on which scrivener you trust. Hospitality and exploitation, I have long suspected, share a thinner wall between them than guests prefer to believe. A bed tendered for metal currency is no scandal; yet the tendering is rarely so altruistic as the placard would have one conclude.

No bedmeister of any acuity runs a roadside boarding house out of pure charity, and Noseman Under Eaves is, by the pinch of his scapulae, a man of acuity. He wants money, as most do. I begrudge him not that general feature.

Howbeit, I would put the question gently: is an exchange of square gold pieces truly the most accomodating currency between two practitioners of any consequential craft? Coins, once paid, terminate the obligation; coins free both parties from one another, and in freeing them limits what each may yet request of the other. An oath, by contrast, tethers the twain in a manner more enduring, more productive, and frequently more salutary.

## 6. ESSAY

The wandering scholar is a serviceable cover precisely because it is, in my case, no full untruth; the trick of such imposture lies in selecting which true thing to disclose. In this instance I presented 'dream-mancy' as a dead specialism, no licensed practitioner of the discipline remaining and no apprentice known to me by repute, and the claim passes the test of accuracy in spirit, if not in most rigorous fact. The field has produced its odd survivors, of course, and well it may be that I have missed some orphan.

Thusly introduced, I stood and waited. One ought always to let the seller name his terms first. Bedmeister Noseman Under Eaves kept his lips still also.

A mercenary there was at The Hooded Pillow that day, whose name was Weirmoth, sitting at the bar in a cloak patched from salvaged funeral shrouds. I single him out particularly at this juncture for it was he who proposed the eventual arrangement: that he and I ride at dawn for the bluff, and resolve whatever there was within which afflected the village so. I made no particular demand for my assistance, only asserting my right to 'recompense', and to decide upon its form, should my talents prove essential to the task.

## 9. ESSAY

The Tower of the Attercop sits upon its bluff above Sulky Wend like a skeleton articulated to receive visitors; bleached in the slate-colored morning light, it gives no impression until one is at the door. Upon that door rests the Seal of Neinstitch, a sutured lock which answers only to a certain murmur.

I should say, lest the reader misconstrue, that the "unsealing" itself demand no act of violation, physical or otherwise. The real strength of necromancy is knowledge, not force; a lock of this variety wishes to be opened by the proper syllable, and resents only the unproper one. Hammer and pick would accomplish nothing here save the shame of the wielder.

The Tower itself was raised by a cabal calling itself 'The Red Pew', and was breached, or fabled to have been breached, by a rival fellowship of the name 'Milkmoot'. Howsoever the breach went, the seal was set after.

## 12. LEMMA — The Tower of the Attercop

![IMAGE: a grey silked stone tower on a windswept bluff, web-patina glittering on every cornice, a single sutured iron door at its base](images/the-tower-of-the-attercop.png)

**Site:** Bluff above the hamlet of Sulky Wend, Road of Graves.
**Use:** Sealed chrypt and working-house, formerly held by the cabal called The Red Pew.
**Plan:** Upper halls, scriptorium of three floors, still-room with copper coils, cauldron chamber at the base.
**Patina:** Every interior surface bears a film of attercop silk, cultivated within the walls and thickened by the slow seep of preservative gas from the stoppered cauldron.
**Status:** Sealed three thousand-odd nights by rival cabal Milkmoot. Reputed unbreached until recent unsealing.

## 14. ESSAY

Nailed to a column in the entry-hall is a preserved ledger, names ranked beside duty-hours, the latter columns marking shifts that ended long before any of the present sleepers in the village were born. Each name was a person; each person, in turn, a precise constellation of tissues, and I find myself wondering, as the verdigris gas pools at my ankles, which tissues the gas has preserved best. The hand bones, peradventure. The cartilage of the ear.

There is, on the lectern by the stair, another tableau worth attending to: three common books open to the same page, spines cracked, titled On the Conduct of Sealed Houses, Compendium of Threshold Speech, and A Practitioner's Almanac. Knowledge bound in calf will eventually crumble; tissue, by some quieter logic, renews itself in cycles, even if the cycle requires the gas of a stoppered cauldron to keep its rhythm. I am not sure which is the more honest archive.

And then, lower down, the muscle-wall: a weave of fibrous flesh strung across the passage, both seal and tumor, the Tower's last immune response and, withal, a candid symbiosis of the Artificial and the Biological. I think it knows I am here. I concede that this last claim is fanciful; yet the wall, I suspect, would not protest the compliment.

## 16. ESSAY

The Scriptorium opens in three floors around a central balcony, a dead and dim false-sun globe presiding from above; the gas has put a verdigris patina on every binding, and the codices on the upper shelves are moldering at a rate I would estimate as merely indecent rather than catastrophic. Most of these texts will outlast the village below, which is not a high bar, but the bar is what we have.

One volume I will name, for my own future record: Potions and Poisons of Eleven Reagents, in fair condition, half-read. The price of that book alone, in any honest market, would purchase Sulky Wend twice over and have copper enough left to buy Weirmoth thrice, with his spear and his canary thrown in. I do not say this with relish; I say it as appraisement.

The provenance of these works troubles me, somewhat. Most of what I see here was authored by hands no extant register can claim; some, however, bear marks of a quite recent age, which is not the answer the chronicles would have me expect. I shall not press the point further in this entry. There will be time, perhaps, to press it later.

## 18. ESSAY

The gas, which the village has taken to calling the dreamreel for reasons of its own, issues from the cauldron in the chamber below; the cauldron is stoppered, but stoppering is a porous discipline, and certain of the eleven reagents seep outward in vapor, lodging in the sleep of any unwary soul within a half-day's ride. What the villagers report as death-visions are, I think, simply the memories of the dead pressing through cloth too thin to hold them.

To pass the muscle-wall below, one must brew a decoction of eleven reagents, of which I have ten by name and provenance: Widowwine, Priest's Quicklime, Honey, Green from the Blades of Grass, Black Lye, Glasswort, Crystalwort, Iron shavings, Dandelion root, and Preserved nine-day-gnats. The eleventh the book names only as Respiration from a Silver Dream, and it must be drawn from a memory; the book, in customary fashion, declines to be more specific.

A second potion permits a second party to enter that memory. Howbeit, the second potion is unsafe to any practitioner habituated to necromantic energies, of whom I am unhappily one; the unhabituated may drink it without cost. Weirmoth qualifies; I do not. The arrangement, I concede, is convenient.

## 21. ESSAY

I stand over Weirmoth where he sleeps on the brewing-bench, listening to the murmured names that surface in his throat; the names are not his. Some, I am almost certain, are mine, or were once. I find myself returning, unbidden, to the night of the breach: the rival cabal at the gates, the colleagues calm in their chairs, the long lever of brass beneath my then-living hand. I closed the seal. I closed it without grief, though not without thought, and the absence of grief is, I now think, the part of the act that ought most to trouble me.

I would call it cowardice rather than crime, for the act was cerebral and admitted of no rage; yet the distinction may be less consoling than I have permitted myself to find it. Weirmoth, in his borrowed sleep, is at this very moment witnessing the betrayal as the cabalists witnessed it, walking my old corridors with the ingredient I lack. When his face slackens by a degree, I bend close, whisper the Tongue Disinterred, and lift the knowledge from him as one lifts a coin from a basin.

The decoction of eleven reagents I then brew at the bench, one-handed and steady, in the customary order. Beside it I prepare another mixture, of a kind I have not yet declared; that, perhaps, is for a later entry.

## 25. LEMMA — The Lidd Decoction

![IMAGE: a stoppered glass phial of pale grey liquid steaming faintly, lashed beside a sealed brewing cauldron](images/the-lidd-decoction.png)

A cauldron-nullifying poison brewed by the mancer Othelmedir, named for the surname he bore before his exile. Intended for the vessel, not the flesh: poured into a stoppered cauldron through its rim-seams, the decoction is said to resolve preserved contents to inert muck within the span of a long sigh, ending any leak of the cauldron's vapor. Effect on living tissue is undocumented and presumed severe.

## 26. ESSAY

What follows here is the brief account of the Cauldron Chamber as it transpired, given without ornament, for the chamber itself has had ornament enough in its long sleep. Weirmoth made his position known at the threshold: he would not interfere, the bargain still holding, and I had no cause to doubt him on that count, whatever else I may have doubted. I emptied the Lidd Decoction into the cauldron; there was a hiss, followed by a longer sigh, as of something at last being permitted to exhale.

I intoned deadspeech, the necessary syllables and no more, until the contents resolved to muck and went still; and then, in the order proper to such withdrawals, I yielded the stolen reagents jar by jar back to their shelves. Weirmoth watched without comment. I had forgotten, methinks, how steady a man with a spear can be when he has chosen to be steady.

The pendant of hammered tin, lidded cauldron sigil and single open eye, I retained; he had forgotten it, and I did not remind him. The omission was, I think, defensible. There are debts the road will press for later, and one prefers to have the ledger weighted in one's own favor when it does.
