# General description of black book blob styles

4-5 Different blob styles:
- **verse** - like a series of modern text messages, but written by a learned necromancer in Ashton-Smithian dialogue; *replacement for ACTION*
- **essay** - like Dario's 'Machines of loving grace' AI essays; *replacement for DESCRIPTION*
- **interview** - Necromancer poses brief question fragments, character gives dialogue answer; always preceded by a header with character name and optional circumstances; *replacement for DIALOGUE*
- **Memorials** - exactly how it sounds - like epitaphs or memorials on a gravestone; always a sequence of a few in a row; *replacement for REFLECTION*
- **Lemma** - A single encyclopedia entry: monsters, locations, spells, diseases, items, whatever - possibly, always accompanied by an image; *replacement for TABLE*

## Interview

Voice: Conversational, candid, often colloquial — the respondent's natural speaking register, with verbal tics, asides, and digressions preserved. Think transcribed speech more than composed prose.
POV: First-person (the respondent), with an implied second-person frame — the bolded header stands in for an unseen interviewer. The reader eavesdrops rather than being addressed directly.
Tense: Anchored in the present (the moment of answering), with past-tense excursions for anecdote and occasional future for speculation. Mixed, but the "now" of the interview is the stable ground.
One-line: A framed monologue where bolded prompts stand in for an unseen interviewer, and the subject answers in candid, present-tense first-person speech.

```
Example:
**Sources of inspiration?**
[Respondant's answer - IE. something like, "Well I always loved westerns when I was a kid. My dad..."]
```

## Memorials

Voice: Formal, elevated, and compressed — weight per word is high because it's carved in stone (or meant to feel that way). Tone ranges from eulogistic warmth (Ex. 1) to lapidary stoicism (Ex. 2) to oracular admonition (Ex. 3), but always declarative, never chatty. Think of it like the mason charging by the letter: every syllable has to earn its place.
POV: Either third-person about the deceased (the tribute/eulogy — Ex. 1 & 2) or second-person to the passing reader (the "wayfarer address" — Ex. 3). Occasionally first-person from the dead themselves ("As I am, so shall ye be"). First-person from the living writer is rare.
Tense: Past tense for the biography of the dead; present tense for the verdict, the toast, or the wisdom aimed at the reader. The two often sit side by side in a single inscription.
One-liner: Condensed commemorative prose — aphoristic and declarative, aimed either at honoring the dead or addressing the living who pass by.

```
Example 1:
## The Old Soulwood
A named forest on the Road of Graves, predating the Time of Dying. A demon king willed his field to his human slave concubine on death. The necromancer-gods, unable to kill a human, drove the Road of Graves through her land instead. She planted magic juniper berries atop the first epitaphs; these rooted into stone and spread into an unstoppable forest. The Bouldsatr later settled it before being hunted to extinction.


Example 2:
## Dragon Eye Longsword
This fabulous weapon’s hilt is the shape of a serpent, with its fangs wrapped around a gleaming opal. It was made by a nameless mancer, who is said to have filled it with the hatred borne against mankind by the last, degenerated dragon. The Great Wyrm of The Leather Dragon is its bearer.

Example 3:
## Periwinkle Forest Cat
Sage Name: Felis animosilva major
Description: Among the largest living species of hunting cat: 4.3-4.5 meters in length, shoulder height of 2.2-2.3 meters, and a reach of about 3 meters. This cat species has unusually long and sinewy limbs, allowing it to spring and run with great agility, and even to climb trees. Its face is flat, with a less-powerful jaw comparative to its size. Typical weight exceeds two tons.
Habitat: The Soulwood and its quarters.
```

## Lemma

Voice: Impersonal, authoritative, encyclopedic — no narrator presence, no "I" or "you," no emotional coloring. Hedges toward the scholarly with phrases like "is said to have" when attesting to legend vs. fact.
POV: Third-person omniscient/objective. The writer is an invisible cataloger who simply knows.
Tense: Mixed, governed by time-relation to the entity:

Present for enduring description / ongoing existence ("hilt is the shape of a serpent," "Typical weight exceeds two tons").
Past for origins and history ("was made by a nameless mancer," "drove the Road through her land").

One-liner: Terse, omniscient reference-prose that catalogs an entity's essential nature, origin, and place in the world as if transcribed from a worldly encyclopedia.
