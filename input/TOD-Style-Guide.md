# Style Guide: Time-of-Dying

> **POV:** Third-person limited

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average:** 15 words. **Range:** 2–56 words. **Std. dev.:** ~10 words.
- **Distribution:** Bimodal. Short declarative punches (2–10 words) against a middle band (10–20 words), with a long tail (30+) for accumulative sentences. Typical texture: short-short-short-long.

### 1.2 Sentence Types

- Simple (single independent clause): 39%
- Compound (coordinated independent clauses): 26%
- Complex (independent + dependent): 24%
- Compound-complex: 11%

Achieve complexity *between* sentences, not within them.

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- Subject-first (SVO): 56%
- Conjunction opener (And, But, For): 12% — "And" dominates; chronicle-like additive cadence
- Prepositional or adverbial opener: 12%
- Dependent clause opener: 6%
- Participial phrase opener: 4%
- Inverted or verb-first: 4%
- Other (fragments, demonstratives, temporal markers): 6%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- Typical clause count per sentence: 1–3.
- Method: Right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas, semicolons, or "and." Subordination is shallow. Deep stacking uses appositional layering, not syntactic embedding.
- Maximum comfortable depth: 3–4 clauses. Sentences grow wide, not deep.

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average:** 3–4 sentences, ~43 words. **Range:** 1–11 sentences.
- **Distribution:** Front-loaded toward short paragraphs. Single-sentence paragraphs common for action beats and dialogue. Catalog set-pieces run longer.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Opening move:** Action or concrete perception. Never a topic sentence.
- **Closing move:** Striking terminal image or clipped declarative. The most vivid element comes last; final sentences tend to be the paragraph's shortest.
- **Rhythm:** Accumulative build, then snap shut with a short sentence. Catalogs brake with a final sentence.

### 2.3 Transitions

- **Dominant method:** Direct continuation of action or dialogue. Scene shifts via brief marker: "presently," "one morning," "dawn came."
- **Hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 34% hard / 66% linked.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register and Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** ~40/60 Germanic-dominant. Germanic monosyllables carry action and physical description; Latinate vocabulary used selectively for atmosphere and the numinous.
- **Register:** Elevated-precise. Plain Germanic chassis with selective Latinate ornamentation.
- **Archaism:** Occasional vocabulary flavoring, not structural. Examples: "rapier," "doffed," "bereft," "manse," "unguent." Pronouns and verb conjugations stay modern. Invents formality more than he borrows it.

### 3.2 Specificity and Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract concepts appear only in dialogue or sorcerous thought.
- **Noun specificity:** Maximum. "Rapier" not "sword," "crags" not "rocks," "pelgrane" not "creature."
- **Verb specificity:** Very high and kinetic. "Lurched," "strode," "pranced," "clambered," "snatched." Verbs encode manner and sensation; adverbs are largely unnecessary.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** ~1.4
- **Polysyllabic frequency (3+ syllables):** ~9%
- **Placement:** Clause-end or sentence-end for rhythmic weight. Each polysyllable registers. Invented proper nouns carry much of this load.

### 3.4 Repetition and Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Structural when present. Key-word echo for thematic anchoring ("strange soil… strange air… strange and new"), anaphoric "And" in catalog passages, verbatim repetition of ritual phrases. Serves accumulation or motif, not decoration.
- **Synonym variation:** Low-to-moderate. Character names and key nouns reused; verb choices varied freely.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating new words, apply these filters in order:

1. **Etymology:** Default to short Germanic roots for action and physical description. Use Latinate or exotic roots for atmosphere and the numinous.
2. **Syllable target:** Favor monosyllables and disyllables (~91%). Reserve trisyllabic+ words for clause-end rhythmic weight. Never more than one per sentence.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in 1950s literary adventure-fantasy for an educated audience? Reject journalism, modern-casual prose, and academic jargon.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier or rarer words at clause-end or sentence-end. Keep sentence openings blunt and Germanic.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjectives:** Pre-noun dominant. Paired modifiers common ("dark trees," "grave blue eyes"). Clusters of three+ are rare. Occasional post-noun placement for archaic flavor.
- **Density:** 1–2 per noun phrase. Many noun phrases pass unmodified. One exact modifier over several vague ones.
- **Adverbs:** Pre-verb, sentence-initial, or mid-clause. Never stacked. Frequency: ~1 per 5–6 sentences. Manner encoded in verb choice instead.

### 4.2 Inversion and Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional signature flavor.
- **Types:** Verb-subject inversion in dialogue tags ("said T'sais"), object-fronting for emphasis, predicate-adjective fronting, scenic reveals ("Ahead shone a glimmering sea").
- **Trigger context:** Dialogue tags (almost always inverted), scenic reveals, wonder, horror, elevated passages. Inversion functions as a spotlight.

### 4.3 Apposition and Interjection

- **Appositives:** Frequent. Core device for identification, recharacterization, and world-building. Typical position: mid-sentence between commas, or post-noun. Can stack into mini-catalogs.
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare. Em dashes for dramatic insertions; commas for lighter qualifications; parentheses for encyclopedic asides only.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Structural. One of the most recognizable markers.
- **Forms:** Tricolon constructions, anaphoric catalog lists ("And one… And another…"), paired clauses, serial verb chains, balanced antithesis in dialogue, polysyndetic coordinate lists.
- **Default list length:** 3 items (tricolon). Catalogs may extend to 6–8 items for world-building inventory.

---

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- ~0.5 figures per paragraph. 3–5 per page. Many paragraphs contain none. Figures land hard because of their rarity.

### 5.2 Type Preference

- Simile: 38%
- Metaphor (embedded in verb or noun choice): 27%
- Personification: 22%
- Synecdoche or metonymy: 10%
- Other: 3%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. Light, color, and fire
2. Geology, architecture, and ruin
3. Body, anatomy, and sensation
4. Animals, predation, and the organic
5. Sleep, death, water, and weather

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Simile form:** Brief "like X" or "as X," 2–5 words. One vehicle, no elaboration.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb and noun choice rather than stated. The verb carries the freight ("the sun ballooned upward," "coppered her skin"). This is the dominant figurative mode.
- **Length:** Single phrase or single verb. Extended conceits are absent.

---

## 6. Punctuation

- **Commas:** ~2 per sentence average. Oxford comma consistent. Comma splices are a deliberate acceleration device.
- **Semicolons:** ~1 per 10 sentences. Heavier in catalog and expository passages. Joins parallel independent clauses; separates items in complex lists.
- **Em dashes:** Moderate frequency. Appositional insertion, dramatic interruption or reveal, compressed identifying phrases, interrupted speech.
- **Colons:** Rare. Introduces dramatic revelation or formal elaboration.
- **Ellipses:** Rare; trailing speech more often marked by em dash. When present, spaced dots (. . .).
- **Exclamation marks:** Dialogue only, reserved for genuine alarm or theatrical intensity. Absent in narration.
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare. Free indirect discourse ("Was this beautiful?") or rhetorical use.

---

## 7. Rhythm and Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic with trochaic interruptions at sentence openings and spondaic clusters for gravity ("sprang back," "black dust"). Dactylic variation in description.
- **Regularity:** Controlled enough to feel shaped; varied enough to avoid sing-song. Shaped by ear, not metrical.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration:** Short declarative sentences, asyndeton, comma-spliced compound predicates, dropped subordination, stacked dialogue without tags.
- **Deceleration:** Coordinate "and" chains, polysyndeton, embedded appositives, cataloged parallel phrases, trailing subordinate chains.
- **Paragraph shape:** Steady accumulative build, then snap shut with a blunt final sentence.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional, unforced. Background texture, not foregrounded.
- **Assonance or consonance:** Occasional. Woven into key phrases and proper nouns.
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare. Verbs carry sonic work ("gasped," "thudded," "rasping").

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Frequency:** Most lines tagged. Tagless exchanges for clear two-speaker contexts.
- **Preferred tag verb:** "Said" dominates (~60%), "asked" for questions. Expressive alternatives earned by context. No decorative tags.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant with frequent verb-subject inversion ("said T'sais"). Mid-speech tags for longer turns. Pre-speech tags are rare.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Turn length:** 15–20 words for exchanges; up to 100+ words for expository monologue or legend-telling. Asymmetry between mentor-orator and student-questioner is characteristic.
- **Interruption:** Rare. Marked by em dash. Characters speak in complete, controlled utterances.
- **Idiolect markers:** Invented rather than mimetic. Distinct verbal registers via rhythm, attitude, and word selection. Authority figures use longer constructions with weary formality. Naïve characters use short declaratives. Villains are theatrical and self-aware.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- ~40% dialogue / 60% narration in dialogue-containing passages. Ratio shifts by scene. Dialogue arrives in concentrated bursts, then recedes.

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- Visual: 68%
- Tactile or kinesthetic: 14%
- Auditory: 13%
- Olfactory: 3%
- Gustatory: 2%

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium (room-scale or clearing-scale). Landscape-scale for travel passages.
- **Movement:** Follows the acting character or pans slowly. Close-ups for faces and key details. Catalog scenes inventory the space, pausing on each figure for one identifying detail.
- **Focal priority:** Environment-first in establishing shots, character-first in dialogue and action.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Pure description before action resumes:** 1–4 sentences on average; up to 5–8 in set-piece catalogs.
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into forward motion. Once a scene is set, description arrives as appositional inserts dropped into action sentences. Front-loads description, then thins it as action takes over.

---

## 10. Usage Notes

1. **Sentence construction:** Alternate short punches (3–12 words) with medium compounds (15–25 words). Reserve long accumulative sentences (30+) for catalogs. Default to SVO openings (~56%). Use conjunction openers ("And") ~12%. Keep subordination shallow; favor right-branching coordination. Target the 39/26/24/11 type ratio.

2. **Word selection:** Run candidates through the five-step filter in §3.5. Germanic monosyllables are the backbone; Latinate polysyllables are color accents. Choose the most specific concrete noun and the most kinetic verb. If you need an adverb, the verb is wrong.

3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep most paragraphs to 2–4 sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs function as rhythmic punctuation. Open with action or image. Close on a concrete image or the shortest, sharpest sentence in the paragraph. One extended catalog paragraph per major scene.

4. **Figurative language:** ~1 figure per 2–3 paragraphs. Embed metaphor in verb choice. Similes use "like X" in 2–5 words. Draw from light, landscape, body, and decay. Never extend beyond one sentence.

5. **Parallelism and catalog:** Deploy tricolons for gravity. Use "And one… And another…" for crowd inventory. One detail per figure.

6. **Punctuation:** Commas for pacing and serial verb chains; periods for force. Semicolons for balanced parallel clauses. Em dashes for appositional insertion and dramatic reveals. Exclamation marks for sharp dialogue only. Spaced ellipses (. . .) for trailing implication.

7. **Dialogue:** Invert tags ("said T'sais"). Use "said" or "asked" for the majority; expressive tags only when precise. Lightly elevated register, formal but natural. Keep speeches short (15–20 words). Differentiate characters by rhythm, attitude, and verbal register.

8. **Rhythm check:** Read aloud. Short sentences land like struck bells. Long ones accumulate mass through additive coordination. Paragraphs should build to a culminating image, then snap shut. If a sentence ends on a soft polysyllable, restructure it.

9. **Tone:** Cool precision underwritten by melancholic wonder. Detached, faintly sardonic clarity. The narrator observes with a lapidary eye and never editorializes. Violence rendered flatly; absurdity juxtaposed without comment. Never sentimental, never cynical. Late afternoon light: warm but fading.

10. **Encyclopedic asides:** When introducing creatures, places, or historical events, drop briefly into a dry, authoritative, almost clinical tone as though quoting from an in-world reference text. Then snap back to the action without transition.
