# Dreamreel Scene 1 (claude's version)

And the road into Sulky Wend ran low between hedges of dead bracken, and the wind off the road came in with Othelmedir when he stooped beneath the lintel of *The Hooded Pillow*, and stayed afterward in the wool of his cloak. The lintel was set too low for a man of his height; he stood tall as a redwood and thin as a reed, iron eyes set in a broad dark face, cropped curls and salt-and-pepper beard; and the door had been rehung on hinges fixed to the wrong jamb, as if the builder had worked from memory of a building he had only heard described. He noted the error without remarking upon it. He noted, too, the smell that met him within: stale millet, unwashed wool, the close ferment of bodies that had not bathed in a week of evenings. He drew the cloak closer at the throat and let the dead weight of his left arm settle against his ribs beneath the oxblood velvet, where no one need see it. He stood a moment in the doorway and looked. He had lived near Sulky Wend, once, though rarely come into the town.

And the room beyond the doorway lay slumped under its own low ceiling, the hearth a heap of orange coals giving more glow than warmth, the benches against the long wall populated by men and a woman or two who slept upright in their clothing. On the nearest bench a boy of perhaps fourteen sat with his head fallen back against the plaster, jaw slack, and Othelmedir, while letting his eyes pass across the company, marked the boy's neck for its breadth and the white evenness of the boy's teeth where the lower lip had drawn aside. He marked these as a man marks coin in a purse. At the boy's elbow a tallow stub burned in a clay dish, the wick black-curled and bent, the flame so reduced that it lit only the cup beside it.

And at the far end of the room, beyond the line of sleeping bodies, the bedmeister stood at his plank counter beneath an iron tally of bone-coloured beads strung on a nailed cord, and was speaking to a client who had come in before. The voice carried thinly across the room.

"Show your coin. How many nights?"

"Two."

"Show the second hand. Any cough?"

The client coughed once, then shook his head. The beads on the tally clacked along the cord under the bedmeister's thumb. From the corner of the room, where Othelmedir had not yet looked, a frost radiated.

He turned his head. The frost was a hand's-breadth from a child of seven or eight, barefoot, in a grey shift of coarse wool, who stood between two of the benches with her eyes open and unfixed, and the frost did not extend beyond her. He could feel it on the side of his face as he might have felt the draught of a cellar door. She drew breath and began.

"One. Lamp of the unlit hour."

"Two. Quiet for the broken oath."

"Three. Bread without the salt."

The bedmeister, behind her and not seeing her, said, "Pallet or bed? Strawn or feather?"

"Four. Door without the name."

"Five. Hand without the ring."

And Othelmedir listened to the rite - The Little Mass of Failing Lamps - as a builder listens to the cracking of his own beam, and the dead arm beneath the velvet seemed colder than the rest of him.

The bedmeister looked up from his tally and saw him, and lifted a hand. "You there. Bed or bench?"

Othelmedir crossed between the tables, passing the boy and the untouched pewter cup whose surface bore a thin grey film, and came to stand at the plank counter where the ledger lay open. The book was bound in attercop silk, oilskin-grey, dulled and ridged at the spine where many fingers had worn it, and its open page showed names entered in a careful hand, each name struck through with one short line.

"You ask, master bedmeister, whether I take bed or bench. Let it be a bed, if any room remains, and let it be the highest room you keep, as I prefer the quiet of an upper floor."

The bedmeister's thumb moved along the cord.
