# Dreamreel Scene 3 (claude's version)

The bluff path went up out of Sulky Wend in switchbacks, slate breaking thinly under the horses' shoes, brittle grass laying flat where the slope tilted forward and the footing tried to give downhill against them. Othelmedir rode at the front. His oxblood cloak was darkened with the morning's wet, and his white prosthetic left arm hung knuckle-jointed in his lap, a thing of carved bone with iron pin-joints that took no oath from the reins.

Weirmoth came up behind, his spear butt riding against his stirrup, the point dragging now and then a thin furrow in the path's dust, the kind of furrow a child draws with a stick and forgets. Below them the village was already gone under mist that did not lift but only thickened among the pines, as though the valley had taken a long breath and forgotten to give it back.

Othelmedir did not look down. He had been told by Bedmeister Noseman that one approached the Tower of the Attercop from the east in any weather; he had set the rule himself, years gone, under another name, and was old enough now to be obeyed by his own old habits.

They dismounted at the foot of two stone steps cut into the bluff-face, where the chrypt-door waited oak-black under its iron banding, and at the door's center a brass seal hung in a circular bezel, its engraving worn so thin by weather that only the outermost rim still kept the old shape of letters. Othelmedir stood before it. His good hand he laid against the brass, palm-flat, and the prosthetic he let hang.

Weirmoth held the horses at a few paces and squinted past him, not flinching, the cadence of the seal-work landing on him like a word he had once known.

"You ride this kind of work without a blade on you, friend?" Weirmoth said. "Not even a paring knife in the boot. A man might catch his death of nothing in particular."

"Weirmoth, a blade has no quarrel with what feeds inside this door, and I do not carry what I cannot use."

"Each man to his own poison, and mine's the long iron."

Othelmedir half-turned his face. "If we let what is inside continue, the people in the valley below will not, by the third moon, be themselves any longer; the dead replace the living from inside, memory by memory. There were two cabals here once, the Red Pew and the Milkmoot, and the Red Pew is what dreams now in Sulky Wend's beds."

"Heard those names before in a tavern, maybe. Or maybe the names are new and I've a tavern's head on my shoulders." Weirmoth scratched at his sideburn. "Slept rough last night, truth told, and that's no usual sleep for me."

"You told me once that you never dreamed at all, that the inside of your skull was a winter church with the doors shut."

"Said a lot of things. The mouth runs on its own."

Othelmedir turned back. He bent his head close to the brass. The whisper he gave was so small that Weirmoth, three paces off, heard it only as a ringing pressure behind his own jaw: *Öltesoldzo sjhaj*. With a sigh came loose the sutures.

A seam parted along the door's lower edge. From it came pouring a slow green-grey heaviness, gas with weight in it, that did not rise but went over the sill and down the steps in the manner of something poured. Behind the door cobwebs began to quiver all at once, the whole inside of the chrypt shivering through its silks though no wind had been let in.

They withdrew up the steps and stood out of the path of the spill. Inside, beyond the door's parted seam, a fat pale spider lowered itself on a single bright thread toward the green and stopped, swaying, unwilling to drop the last hand's-breadth.

Weirmoth sank onto a cold stone block beside the path, the damp coming up through his trousers. "Seminary town," he said, to nothing in particular. "Grew up past the low fens. Different kind of fume." He fished from his cloak a preserved canary the color of old wax, one glass bead for an eye and the other socket empty.

"Mister Ribbon," he told it, soft, "this throat we're earning today is more than coppers in the cup, eh, more than coppers in the cup." His collar lay turned, the seams reversed there, small and even, the work of patient hands. He did not look at Othelmedir at all.

Othelmedir watched him, and said nothing.
