# Dreamreel Scene 2 (claude's version)

The Hooded Pillow kept its evening as Othelmedir read it: the common room bedded down to a single hearth whose coal threw a low oxblood glow without anything that could be honestly called heat, a draught moving beneath the warped door and running cold along the ankles of those still upright, smelling of wet ash and of the ash beneath the ash. He had taken his place at the plank counter and there remained, unhurried, the velvet of his cloak laid on a gnarl of the wood, while at the bench-end nearest the door a smaller man held forth to the bedmeister in low, busy tones, as if their bargain had been an old one resumed and not a fresh thing being struck for the first time. The bedmeister listened in the way of men who have not slept fully in many weeks; he listened, that is, with the whole of a body that wished only to lie down. Othelmedir did not turn to either of them. He let his gaze move across the room as a valuer moves across a room: the man slumped in the corner seat, thick-necked, the cords of him sound even in sleep; the girl on the bench, her teeth caught in the low light, even and white, the jaw well-set. He waited, as travellers are taught to wait in houses where the price has not yet been named, and he allowed the room to speak before the room's keeper did.

"How long's it lain on you?" Weirmoth said, and laid the sliver of fat-soap on the counter beside his mug, where a small, sweating ring of grease had already begun to mark the wood; he turned a hand over, examined the pale crescent of a nail, and looked up again as if the question were a passing matter of the road.

"Three months. Maybe four." Noseman's thumb went along the attercop-silk cord of the ledger; it was fraying, where the thumb had gone for years.

"Run me through the ailment."

The bedmeister breathed out. "The living dream the dead. We dream how they died. Each night, again." His eyes were lower-lidded with broken red lacework, the iris half-lost in a yellowed white.

"Of which dead does the village dream, and by what reckoning have you marked them upon the ledger?"

"Old men. Old women. Strangers, mostly." He set a finger on the ledger page, where a column of names ran half struck through by a single drawn line. "Names struck out, lined."

"Like a worry behind the eyes?"

"Like a worry, yes. It will not loosen. You try to think elsewhere. A barn. A wife. Some hymn you half remember. The death returns. It crowds out the dream. We call it the Dreamreel."

Against the bench-leg the barefoot child slept on, mouth open, eyelids running with whatever ran behind them; her breath came small and even and seemed not to belong to the words above her at all.

"Coin's on the table, friend. Name what's fair, hand to hand."

"Ride to the Tower. Burn whatever feeds it."

"My spear has done worse errands." Weirmoth tipped his head toward the weapon leaned against the bench-end, its butt wrapped in oiled twine, a hairline split running the length of the haft; the funeral-shroud patches of his cloak caught the lamplight along the reversed seams as he shifted. "It once belonged to a saint who lanced abscesses on a poisoned bishop's throat; it whistles, so they say, whenever sickness is near."

Noseman did not answer this. He passed the cord through his fingers and kept his eyes on the ledger.

Othelmedir, who had not yet been counted in the bargain, spoke from the counter. "If I may take up your figure of the worry, master bedmeister, I would say a worry stops where the worrier sleeps, whereas a Dreamreel does not; that, more than the visions themselves, is what undoes a town."

The two men turned. Weirmoth measured the height of him with the appraising look of a man who has weighed many strangers.

"Othelmedir is the name I carry on these roads," he said, the right hand still upon the counter, the left side of his cloak hanging without a partner in its fall, "a specialist in dream-mancy, lately come up out of the south, and at your service; the discipline is unlicensed now, the last registered practitioners having died without heirs, but the knowledge did not die with the licence. I take no coin; I work by barter and oath, and the terms of those we may set in the morning."

Weirmoth grinned. "A man after my own table. I never dream, myself." He turned the fat-soap over once with a thumb, set it down, and his eyes went to Othelmedir's cloak and the missing left hand that the cloak did not hide. "My back's good for it. Two horses, two backs, at dawn."

Noseman laid three coppers in front of Weirmoth. For an instant something other than sleeplessness moved in his face. He looked down again. "At first light. The horses, ready."

Othelmedir lowered his head a finger's breadth. He crossed to the bench whose edge had been worn shiny by the shoulders of generations of sleepers, and sat.
