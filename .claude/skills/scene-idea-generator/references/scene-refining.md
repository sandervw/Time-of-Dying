# Scene Refining -- Four Questions

For each scene candidate, answer these four questions. Each answer should be 1 sentence, 10-15 words.

## The Questions

### 1. Location & Atmosphere

**Ask:** Where does the scene take place? What does it feel, sound, smell like?

Ground the scene in a specific physical space. Avoid generic locations ("a dark room", "a forest"). Name textures, weather, light quality, ambient sound. The atmosphere should reflect or contrast the scene's tension - a peaceful setting can amplify dread, a chaotic one can mask it.

### 2. Tension & Stakes

**Ask:** What is the central conflict? What's at risk?

Identify the single thing that makes this scene uncomfortable, urgent, or charged. Stakes can be physical (survival, action), social (reputation, loyalty), or psychological (identity, moral). The tension should flow naturally from the collision that generated this scene.

### 3. Key Image

**Ask:** What is one vivid, concrete image or turning point that anchors the scene visually?

This is the moment a reader would remember - the image that would appear on the cover, the frame that would be a painting. It should be specific enough to see clearly and strange enough to stick.

### 4. Movement

**Ask:** What are the characters physically doing? Where are their bodies going?

Characters must never be still -- there is always physical motion within a scene. This can be travel, labor, combat, ritual, or gestures, but bodies must be in motion. Movement reveals character and drives pacing.

## Guidelines

- Let the collision/friction type guide the answers, but don't force it - if a scene wants to drift from its origin friction, let it
- Each question's answer should feel like it belongs to the same scene, not four disconnected fragments
- Prefer the specific and strange over the expected
- Do not introduce new characters beyond what the collision implies unless the scene demands it
