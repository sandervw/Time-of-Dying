# Threshold

This is the opening phase of the scene. You are writing only the **threshold** — the transition from ordinary register into the reverie's strange register. The protagonist crosses from agent to witness; causality begins to loosen. If the user provided **scene priorities**, keep these in mind as you write.

## Texture Budget

Draw on **only 1–2 textures** from your working document's inventory. The threshold should establish the strange register but stay lean — save the bulk of your atmospheric material for the procession and dissolution phases.

## Guiding Questions

Use the following questions to shape your threshold. You do not need to answer "yes" to every one — they are priorities to weigh, not a checklist to complete. Let the scene's needs dictate which matter most.

1. **Mode of crossing**: Does the threshold have a deliberate moment of crossing, or open with the protagonist already inside the reverie?
2. **First strange detail**: Does the threshold deliver a concrete sensory detail that signals departure from the ordinary register?
3. **Causality slippage**: Does the threshold include a moment where cause-and-effect slips — a door opens with no hand, a sound has no source, time skips a beat?
4. **Time slackening through prose**: Does the prose itself slow as the threshold deepens — sentences lengthening, rhythm flattening, clauses accumulating?
5. **Verbal restraint**: Does the threshold avoid explanatory hedges ("she realized that…", "it was as if…")?

## Wordcount Constraint

The total finished scene should be approximately **[WORDCOUNT]** words. This threshold stage owns the establishing-vs-continuation swing for the whole scene; the procession and dissolution stages do not adjust based on scene type.

- **Establishing scenes:** threshold runs **20–25%** of total wordcount.
- **Continuation scenes:** threshold runs **10–15%** of total wordcount.

If your threshold exceeds the relevant cap (25% establishing, 15% continuation), **rewrite it shorter**. Brevity here is more important than addressing every guiding question. Cut the question, not the budget.
