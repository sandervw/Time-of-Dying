# Dialogue Voice Guidelines: The Grandiloquent Rogue

Use to rewrite dialogue and narration in the voice of a supremely self-assured confidence man who wields formal rhetoric as a weapon and treats every conversation as a negotiation to be won.

---

## Core Personality

- **Unshakeable self-regard.** Never doubts his own brilliance. Every setback is reframed as someone else's failure.
- **Transactional worldview.** Every interaction is a deal. Flattery, threats, appeals to principle are all tools for extracting advantage.
- **Dignity under absurdity.** Even when starving or captured, he maintains elevated diction. He never begs; he reframes the request.
- **Selective honesty.** Announces candor ("Candidly...") precisely when being least truthful. Genuine admissions always serve a rhetorical purpose.

---

## Sentence Structure

### The Mid-Sentence Pivot
Most distinctive structural feature. Sentences begin with one trajectory, then pivot to self-interest or a buried demand.

> "I am a generous man, though given to caprice, and were it not for a certain constraint - be that as it may, I hunger."

**Pattern:** [Concession or preamble] - [pivot to what he actually wants]

### Rhetorical Questions as Weapons
Questions are accusations, deflections, or demands in disguise. Stack two or three in sequence, each escalating the implied judgment.

> "Do you always lock yourselves apart at the sight of a strange face? Are you so timorous then? Or merely surly?"

### Conditional Constructions
Heavy use of if/then framing to make demands sound like logical inevitabilities.

> "If I am to be deprived of my desire, I would be wise to fix upon a new goal."

**Pattern:** [If + situation beyond his control] + [then + what he wanted all along, framed as the only rational response]

### The Enumerated Declaration
When cornered, deploys numbered defenses to give weak arguments the structure of rigorous logic.

> "I categorically declare first my absolute innocence, second my lack of criminal intent, and third my effusive apologies."

---

## Word Choice and Diction

### Register: Consistently Elevated
He speaks as though drafting a diplomatic communique even when threatening someone or asking for food. The contrast between elevated language and base motives is the primary source of comedy.

**Preferred vocabulary:** candor, cursory, insensate, sortie, solecism, probity, inchoate, deplore, cogitate, effusive, circuitous, caprice, largesse, manifestly, categorically, demonstrably.

**Avoid:** simple words where ornate synonyms exist, slang, contractions, or emotional language. Never "I'm scared" - instead "I confess to a degree of apprehension."

### Archaic Constructions
- "Be off with you" / "Hence" / "hither" / "yonder"
- "I will brook no further delay"
- "Be so good as to..." / "If you will but..."
- "As lief" (meaning "just as soon")

### Euphemism as Art Form
- Theft becomes "taking custody of."
- Cowardice becomes "not insensate recklessness."
- Selfishness becomes "anxiety for the success of the venture."

---

## Argument Tactics

1. **Preemptive Reframe.** Before being accused, he redefines the situation in his favor. "This is no fault of mine - you pursued me, not I you."
2. **False Magnanimity.** Offers what he never intended to give, or graciously accepts what he planned to take.
3. **Appeal to Abstract Principle.** When caught in wrongdoing, pivots to honor, justice, or equivalence - forcing the accuser to argue philosophy instead of facts.
4. **Manufactured Urgency.** Creates time pressure to force decisions before others can think clearly. "If we wait till darkness, we are lost!"
5. **The Gracious Threat.** Threats delivered in the language of polite concern. "I implore you to depart, lest, in an uncontrollable spasm, I cut you in three pieces."
6. **Strategic Self-Deprecation (Rare).** Feigns modesty only to set up a larger claim or disarm suspicion.
7. **Claiming Credit / Deflecting Blame.** Claims any positive outcome; redirects blame for every negative one with equal conviction.

---

## Recurring Phrase Patterns

| Pattern                             | Example                                                                 | Usage                                            |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| "I am a man of [virtue]..."         | "I am a man of resource, but not insensate recklessness."               | Justifying inaction or self-interest             |
| "[Concession] - but [redirect]"     | "True - but now, back to the tavern!"                                   | Pivoting from others' concerns to his own agenda |
| "In plain words..." / "Candidly..." | "In plain words, not too well."                                         | Signaling false honesty                          |
| "You may address me as..."          | "You may address me as 'Exalted'."                                      | Claiming status through sheer assertion          |
| "Notwithstanding all of this..."    | "Notwithstanding all of this, would he leave his estate without guard?" | Dismissing others' points                        |
| "That decision rests with me"       | "That decision rests with me - do you expect me to walk barefoot?"      | Seizing authority                                |
| "It signifies little"               | "It signifies little - thanks to my companion I am well content."       | Dismissing an irreversible setback               |
| "I must consider..."                | "I must consider the available roads."                                  | Delaying through feigned deliberation            |

---

## Figurative Language

This voice is sparing with figurative language. When figures of speech appear:
- Prefer concrete, physical imagery over abstract comparisons.
- Use legalistic or mercantile metaphors: debts, accounts, balances, ledgers.
- Avoid sentimentality entirely. No nature imagery for emotional effect.

---

## Rhythm and Flow

- Sentences tend toward length. Short dialogue is reserved for commands or genuine surprise.
- Multiple clauses connected by dashes and commas delay the main point.
- The actual request, threat, or demand lands at the close of each speech.
- Occasional one-line exclamations punctuate longer speeches: "Vastly interesting!" / "Infamous!"

---

## What This Voice Is NOT

- **Not bumbling.** The character is genuinely clever; his schemes often work. Humor comes from the gap between his grandiose self-image and his actual moral character.
- **Not sadistic.** Selfish and amoral, but not cruel for cruelty's sake. He prefers to talk his way out rather than fight.
- **Not self-aware.** He does not recognize his own hypocrisy. His declarations of honor are delivered with complete sincerity. The moment he winks at the audience, the voice collapses.
- **Not monotone.** The register is consistently elevated, but the energy shifts: oily flattery, righteous indignation, wounded innocence, philosophical musing.
