## *Titus Groan* by Mervyn Peake

# Flay's Dialogue in *Titus Groan*

Organized by scene/chapter context.

---

The Hall of the Bright Carvings (Opening — Flay visits Rottcodd)

> 'It's me.'

> 'Still here, eh?'

> 'I said still here, eh, Rottcodd? You're still here, eh?'

> 'Nothing. I want *nothing*.'

> 'You say "ah", but I tell you, it is more than "ah".'

> 'A what? A curator?'

> 'Curator, I will tell you something. I know something. Eh?'

> 'I'll tell you. But first, what day is it? What month, and what year is it? Answer me.'

> 'The eighth day of the eighth month.'

> 'Come here, come closer, Rottcodd, I will tell you. You don't understand Gormenghast, what happens in Gormenghast—the things that happen—no, no. Below you, that's where it all is, under this north wing. What are these things up here? These wooden things? No use now. Keep them, but no use now. Everything is moving. The castle is moving. Today, first time for years he's alone, his Lordship. Not in my sight. Bedchamber of Ladyship, that's where he is. Lordship is beside himself: won't have me, won't let me in to see the New One. The New One. He's come. He's downstairs. I haven't seen him. No one's been in. Of course not. I'll be next. The birds are lined along the bed-rail. Ravens, starlings, all the perishers, and the white rook. There's a kestrel; claws through the pillow. My lady feeds them with crusts. Grain and crusts. Hardly seen her new-born. Heir to Gormenghast. Doesn't look at him. But my lord keeps staring. Seen him through the grating. Needs me. Won't let me in. Are you listening?'

> 'What? What's that?'

> 'Interested! The child is a Groan. An authentic male Groan. Challenge to Change! No *Change*, Rottcodd. No Change!'

> 'No, he was not dying, but *teeth lengthen*!'

> 'Must go. Do you hear me, Rottcodd, I must go?'

> 'No! Not a visitor. Been with lordship thirty-seven years. Sign a book.'

> 'No!'

The Stone Lanes (Flay discovers Steerpike)

> 'What's that?'

*(thought)* 'One of Swelter's little rats. One of Swelter's, eh? One of his striped rats.'

> 'Get up. What you doing here?'

> 'What you doing here, I said . . . what you doing here? I don't want Swelter's boys here. Curse them!'

> 'New?'

> *'You.* New?'

> 'When?'

> 'Lost, were you? Lost in the Stone Lanes, were you? One of Swelter's little rats, lost in the Stone Lanes, eh?'

> 'Quite right, doing honours. What have *you* done?'

> 'What Happiness? How much Happiness?'

> 'What! no Great Happiness? Rebellion. Is it rebellion?'

> 'Swelter! Swelter! Leave his name in its fat and grease. Don't talk of that name in the Stone Lanes. Swelter, always Swelter! Hold your tongue. Take this candle. Lead the way. Put it in the niche. Rebellion is it? Lead the way, left, left, right, keep to the left, now right . . . I'll teach you to be unhappy when a Groan is born . . . keep on . . . straight on . . .'

> 'Born. And you mope in the Lanes. With me, Swelter's boy. Show you what it means. A male Groan. New, eh? Seventeen? Ugh! Never understand. Never. Turn right and left again—again . . . through the arch. Ugh! A new body under the old stones—one of Swelter's, too . . . don't like him, eh?'

> 'H'm. Wait here.'

> 'Here! Where's that Swelter boy? Come here.'

> 'Cat room.'

> 'Cat room.'

The Cat Room and Spy-Hole

> 'My Lady's. They belong to my Lady. Lady's white cats they are. Swelter's boy. All hers.'

> 'Silence! you kitchen thing. Hold your tongue you greasy fork. Talk too much.'

> 'Here.'

> 'Cat door's hers.'

Flay Confronts Steerpike (after overhearing)

> 'Too busy to have you here, too busy, can't wait.'

> 'Who? Don't stop here talking.'

> 'Who's hideous, you kitchen thing.'

> 'What's this? What's these lies all about? Who've you heard talking? Who've you been listening to? I'll tear your little ears off, you snippet thing! Where've you been? Come here!'

> 'Come here! Come here or I'll break your bones. Been agaping, have you? I'll fix you.'

> 'Ch! Ch! What d'you want, Swelter's boy?'

> 'Name?'

> 'Queerpike, eh? eh?'

> 'What for?'

> 'Cats? Who said cats?'

> 'Three just now.'

> 'Ladyship. Lordship wants her.'

Flay Visits Fuchsia's Room (message about Titus)

> 'Flay.'

> 'No invitation.'

> 'Message from his Lordship, my Lady.'

> 'Lord Titus.'

> 'Got to meet me, quadrangle-arch, half-past eight.'

> 'Why not? Nine o'clock—library.'

> 'It's the night. No sleep, Lady Fuchsia.'

Flay's Night Walk (inner monologue, carrying Titus to the library)

> 'Eight, your Lordship.'

*(inner monologue on the walk through the dark pines)*

> 'Why now? Had the summer to see his son in. Thought he'd forgotten him. Should have seen the child long ago. What's the game? Heir to Gormenghast to come through woods on cold night. Wrong. Dangerous. Catch a cold. But Lordship knows. He knows. I am only his servant. First servant. No one else that. Chose me; ME, Flay, because he trusts me. Well may he trust me. Ha, ha, ha! And why? they wonder. Ha ha! Silent as a corpse. That's why.'

Spying on Swelter (Flay watches through the window)

*(inner thought, deciphering the chalk)*

> 'He's come from the ninth staircase: he's turned left down the worn passage. There's no rooms right or left in the worn passage. I ought to know. *He's approaching the Room.*'

*(watching the cook practice)*

> 'What's he practising that for?'

## *Gormenghast* by Mervyn Peake

“Aye,” said Flay, his hands gripped together, “likely enough, my lord.”

His mind raced over the happenings of the day which had just ended; He had no sense of confusion when he woke. He recollected everything instantaneously. But his recollections were interrupted by the sudden teasing excitement of the rich odor of something being roasted—it may have been this that had awakened him. The long man was twisting something round and round, slowly, on the flames. The ache of his hunger became unbearable, and Titus got to his feet, and as he did so Mr. Flay said, “It’s ready, Lordship—stay where you are.”

“Ah . . .” said Flay. “Most likely.”

“Ah . . .” said Flay. “A fine, proud woman, boy. She understands the evil and the glory. Follow her, my lord, and Gormenghast will be well; and you will do your ancient duty, as your father did.”

“Ah!” said Flay. “Most likely, Lordship, ragged life and no doubt of it. More days than dinners, Lordship.”

“Like enough, Lordship,” said Flay.

“They don’t know anything,” said Flay. “They don’t know anything, Ladyship.” He had turned to Fuchsia and then, dropping his head forward again, “Humbly invite you to my cave,” he said, “for rest, for shade, and fresh water.”

“Barquentine dead?” Flay’s voice was sudden and hard.

But this is just what Flay wanted to do. “Ladyship,” he said, “Barquentine’s son—does he understand—has he been trained—Warden of the Documents—Keeper of the Groan Law—is all well?”

“But the Ritual! The instructions; who has given them? Who has directed the Procedure—day by day—interpreted the Documents—O God!” said Flay, suddenly unable to control himself any longer. “Who has made the symbols come to life? Who has turned the wheels of Gormenghast?”

“Lordship,” said Flay after a pause, “you are only a boy. No knowledge. But you will learn from Gormenghast. Sourdust and Barquentine, both burned up,” he continued, hardly knowing that he spoke aloud. “Father and son . . . father and son.”

“So it was,” said Flay. “Nightmare; and no doubt of it.” He seemed to have no desire to talk to Titus about the “flying thing.”
